rig n.1

[? SE rig, to play the wanton, to romp about; ult. ety. unknown]

1. a wanton, promiscuous woman; thus used as an insult for any woman (see cite 1832).

1575\xe2\x80\x98Mr. S\xe2\x80\x99 Gammer Gurton\xe2\x80\x99s Needle in Whitworth (1997) III iii: Nay, fie on thee, thou ramp, thou rig.
1598E. Guilpin Skialetheia Satire III n.p.: My Muse shall play the rig Once in her dayes, but shee shall quittance thee .
a.1618J. Davies of Hereford Wittes Pilgr. in Works II 49/2: The most voluptuous ouer-wanton Rigge, Proud Plentie, scornes meeke Pieties Woman-hood .
c.1629 \xe2\x80\x98A Pleasant Country Maying Song\xe2\x80\x99 in Rollins Pepys Ballads (1929\xe2\x80\x9332) This pretty maiden waxeth big: / See what \xe2\x80\x99tis ti play the Rig.
1650T. Fuller Pisgah sight of Palestine (1869) 539: Let none condemn them for Rigs, because thus hoiting with boys .
1696Motteux (trans.) Pantagruelian Prognostications (1927) II 694: Those whom Venus is said to rule [...] light skirts, wrigglers, misses, cats, rigs.
1725View of London & Westminster (2nd part) 51: [in a list of prostitutes] Miss Rigg [Is Visited] By a Shopkeeper in the City.
1739 Duke of Montague quoted by Theodore Hook in Odd People \xe2\x80\x98An Honest Practical Joke\xe2\x80\x99 n.p.: Now all my wig-singeing, and nose-blacking exploits, will be completely outdone by the rig [that was the favourite word in the year 1739] I shall run upon this unhappy devil with the tarnished lace [F&H].
a.1796 \xe2\x80\x98O Can Ye Labour Lee, Young Man?\xe2\x80\x99 in Farmer Merry Songs and Ballads (1897) II 259: A stibble rig is easy plough\xe2\x80\x99d / The fallow land is free.
c.1800 \xe2\x80\x98The Blue Lion\xe2\x80\x99 in Holloway & Black (1975) I 31: Such songs, such gigs, such flashy rigs.
1832Satirist (London) 28 Oct. 346/2: Ah, ah, you call me teef; vot do I vant to be teef for. \xe2\x80\x99Tis you be de teef, old Rig.
1836 \xe2\x80\x98Hurrah For An Irishman\xe2\x80\x99s Sprig!\xe2\x80\x99 in Cockchafer 20: Adam was an Irish man, / And Eve, as we twig, / Only notice the rig, / She was made from an Irishmnan\xe2\x80\x99s sprig!

2. a smart, \xe2\x80\x98sharp\xe2\x80\x99 young man.

1791H. Lemoine \xe2\x80\x98Education\xe2\x80\x99 in Attic Misc. 116: At ev\xe2\x80\x99ry knowing rig, in ev\xe2\x80\x99ry gang, / Dick Hellfinch was the pink of all the slang.
1824 \xe2\x80\x98Sonnets for the Fancy\xe2\x80\x99 in Egan Boxiana III 621: A very knowing rig in ev\xe2\x80\x99ry gang, / Dick Hellfinch was the pick of all the slang.

\n In derivatives\n

riggish \n (adj.)\n

lecherous, amorous, lascivious.

1570P. Levins Manipulus Vocabulorum 145/38: Riggish, licentious.
1607Shakespeare Antony and Cleopatra II ii: For vilest things Become themselves in her, that the holy priests Bless her when she is riggish.
1751W. Kenrick Falstaff\xe2\x80\x99s Wedding (1766) II ix: dol: Give me a buss. fal.: Go, Dol, you are riggish.
1867Armagh Guardian 21 June 8/1: It cannot be absolutely necessary, in order to attain a good and respctable old age, to be riggish or jiggish.
1888\xe2\x80\x9394\xe2\x80\x98Walter\xe2\x80\x99 My Secret Life (1966) VII 1391: Voluptuousness, lickerishness, ruttish, riggish, stupration and harlotry, all words found in the dictionary, and all of which I suppose may be classed under the term erotic.

\n In phrases\n

go the rig \n (v.)\n

(UK Und.) to enjoy oneself.

1846Swell\xe2\x80\x99s Night Guide 68: Aye, she lagged him for his life! \xe2\x80\x98Now,\xe2\x80\x99 said she \xe2\x80\x98the bloak has gone in for a buster; he\xe2\x80\x99s been out for many a pelter, so now ve can go the rig.\xe2\x80\x99.
