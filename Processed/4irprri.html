poultice n.

1. (UK society) a high stiff collar, which resembles a medical poultice.

1840Flash Mirror 18: He has got such a slap up assortment of dickies, donky\xe2\x80\x99s ears, frills, fogles, wipes, neck quids, warm poultices for the winter [etc].
1909 (ref. to 1882) in J. Ware Passing Eng. of the Victorian Era.

2. (UK society) a fat woman.

1909 (ref. to 1880) J. Ware Passing Eng. of the Victorian Era.

3. a Bohemian.

1898Binstead & Wells A Pink \xe2\x80\x99Un and a Pelican 182: I was myself regarded as something of a social poultice, which only meant that I had a great disinclination to regular hours and routine drudgery and had developed several traits of character which now go to constitute what is called a \xe2\x80\x98Bohemian\xe2\x80\x99.

4. (Aus.) a large sum of money; a bribe.

1902Sporting News (Launceston, Tasmania) 22 Nov. 3/1: The connections of the stable which shelters the son of Tostig followed the nag, and put a good \xe2\x80\x98poultice\xe2\x80\x99 on him [OED].
1906Sun. Times (Perth) 30 Sept. 4/8: \xe2\x80\x99Er Nabobness in Vic. / Drors a poultice every day / Only fancy livin\xe2\x80\x99 near / Sixty Thousand Quid a Year!
1932Truth (Sydney) 7 Feb. 5/3: [He] had been told by the popular mentor to open the purse and pop a decent poultice on to Ptolemy [...] those ribbed up should bet like \xe2\x80\x98The Watsons\xe2\x80\x99.
1951(con. 1941) E. Lambert Twenty Thousand Thieves 154: It\xe2\x80\x99s only two days to pay day and I\xe2\x80\x99ve got a poultice in that pay-book of mine.
1957 \xe2\x80\x98Nino Culotta\xe2\x80\x99 They\xe2\x80\x99re a Weird Mob (1958) 73: \xe2\x80\x98Reckon \xe2\x80\x99e pulled \xe2\x80\x99im?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98That\xe2\x80\x99s wot I reckon.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Yer can\xe2\x80\x99t prove ut.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Somebody slung in a poultice, I bet.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98They\xe2\x80\x99re all crooked.\xe2\x80\x99.
1957D. Niland Call Me When the Cross Turns Over (1958) 33: I got paid off, and it was a whacking big poultice.
1979Sun-Herald (Sydney) 24 June 143: A bloke who made a poultice in recent weeks when he sold Rupert a quarter of a million Channel Ten shares [OED].
1986R. Beckett Dinkum Aussie Dict. 41: Poultice: If one \xe2\x80\x98puts a poultice\xe2\x80\x99 on something one has invested a serious amount of money (usually on a horse).
1996J. Byrell Lairs, Urgers & Coat-Tuggers 21: And they collect a poultice on the winner, which was Skerry at a succulent 30-1.
2007S. Maloney Sucked In 155: Save the taxpayer the expense of further buggering around. Those forensic tests cost a poultice.

5. (Ulster) an unpleasantly persistent person.

1977(con. 1930s) S. McAughtry Sinking of the Kenbane Head 38: She was brisk and matter-of-fact and she wasn\xe2\x80\x99t a poultice, like the other girls in our street.

6. (UK Und.) a heavy blow.

1906E. Pugh Spoilers 227: I\xe2\x80\x99ve \xe2\x80\x99urt my arm, lucky for you [...] or I\xe2\x80\x99d put such a poultice on you, you\xe2\x80\x99d think it was your birthday.

7. (US Und.) a story designed to deceive.

1929Hostetter & Beesley It\xe2\x80\x99s a Racket! 234: poultice\xe2\x80\x94Story devised to allay a victim\xe2\x80\x99s suspicions, or keep him from becoming suspicious.

8. (US tramp) a dish of bread and gravy.

1927 \xe2\x80\x98Jargon of the Und.\xe2\x80\x99 in DN V 459: Poultice route, A railway which passes through Mormon territory. A poultice is a slice of bread covered with gravy or preserves.
1931Irwin Amer. Tramp and Und. Sl. 150: Poultice.\xe2\x80\x93A dish of bread and gravy. [...] Poultice Route.\xe2\x80\x93Any rail line through Utah, where the inhabitants are generally hospitable, and where bread and gravy is always to be had even though, on account of poverty, meat may be scarce.
1962Ragen & Finston World\xe2\x80\x99s Toughest Prison 813: poultice \xe2\x80\x93 A dish of bread and gravy.

9. (US prison) a sandwich spread with butter and jam or any other two ingredients.

1931\xe2\x80\x934D. Clemmer Prison Community (1940) 334/2: poultice, n. A bread spread with butter and jam or any two types of spread.

10. a mortgage.

1932K.S. Prichard \xe2\x80\x98Dark Horse of Darran\xe2\x80\x99 in Kiss on Lips 184: Mick Mallane [...] sayin\xe2\x80\x99 if the bank wanted his farm, poultice or no poultice, it\xe2\x80\x99d have to go out and take it from him, and he\xe2\x80\x99d be waitin\xe2\x80\x99 for \xe2\x80\x99m with his gun loaded [OED].
1934T. Wood Cobbers 134: Men talked about their blister, or poultice, which means a mortgage, with complacency.
1955N. Pulliam I Travelled a Lonely Land (1957) 237/2: poultice \xe2\x80\x93 a debt, especially a mortgage on a house.

11. (US Und.) a money belt.

1949Monteleone Criminal Sl. (rev. edn).

12. \n (Aus.) a large number.

1986R. Fitzgerald Pushed from the Wings (1989) 70: They say he used to fuck his students. A poultice of students! Lucky bastard.

13. (N.Z.) a very large sandwich with multiple ingredients.

1988McGill Dict. of Kiwi Sl. 87/2: poultice a large sandwich using everything in the fridge or pantry.
2003McGill Reed Dict. of N.Z. Sl. [as cit. 1988].

\n In phrases\n

poultice over the peeper \n (n.)\n [peeper n. (1)]

a blow on the eye.

1909J. Ware Passing Eng. of the Victorian Era.
sling a poultice \n (v.)\n

(Aus.) to offer a bribe.

1957\xe2\x80\x98Nino Culotta\xe2\x80\x99 They\xe2\x80\x99re a Weird Mob (1958) 73: Someone slung in a poultice, I bet.
