drop n.1

1. with ref. to dropping an object.

(a) (UK/US Und.) a confidence trick, spec. ring-dropping; also attrib.

1777J. Fielding Thieving Detected 28: The Drop [...] There is not a fair in England but some of these villains are to be found at it, and so successful are they in their nefarious schemes, that five hundred pounds are as soon got by them.
1812Vaux Vocab. of the Flash Lang. in McLachlan (1964) 237: drop the game of ring-dropping is called the drop.
1823Egan Grose\xe2\x80\x99s Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue [as cit. 1812].
1837A. Greene Glance at N.Y. I i: Ha! ha! ha! duped again! They have initiated you into the drop business!
1846N.Y. Herald 28 May 2/4: A young man [...] was arrested [...], charged with coming the \xe2\x80\x98drop\xe2\x80\x99 with a pocketbook containing some worthless bank notes.
1860Atlantic Monthly Dec. 671: Dog-smudging, ring-dropping, watch-stuffing [...] are all terms which have more or less outgrown the bounds of their Alsatia of Thieves\xe2\x80\x99 Latin and are known of men [DA].
1933\xe2\x80\x98Goat\xe2\x80\x99 Laven Rough Stuff 40: These were a certain drop to a Mexican, a Westerner, or a foreigner.
1992D. Pinckney High Cotton (1993) 138: \xe2\x80\x98Those flimflams are at the Drop again.\xe2\x80\x99 The aim of the scam was to switch their fake bankroll for your real money, or steal your money outright, if they thought you couldn\xe2\x80\x99t run fast enough.

(b) (UK Und.) a confidence trickster.

1848Flash Dict. in Sinks of London Laid Open 105: Drops, who go about to public houses to cheat unwary countrymen at cards.

(a) (Aus./UK prison, also the long drop) the gallows, thus by meton. execution by hanging.
