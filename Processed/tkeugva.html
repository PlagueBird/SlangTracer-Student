chop n.2

[orig. W.Afr. pidgin, where colonists and Africans alike used it to describe indigenous food. It was further suggested that orig. chop had meant only one dish, long pig, human flesh]

1. food; thus small chop, small items of food.

1805H. Nicholls letter 15 Feb. in Hallet Rec. Afr. Assoc. (1964) xi 208: Their food is chop made of yam cut in slices, cayenne pepper, palm oil, and fowl, fish, goat or wild hog [OED].
1821\xe2\x80\x98An Amateur\xe2\x80\x99 Real Life in London I 197: Let us proceed directly to Dolly\xe2\x80\x99s, take our chop, then a rattler, and hey for the Spell.
1830 \xe2\x80\x98Song of the Steam Coachman\xe2\x80\x99 in C. Hindley James Catnach (1878) 220: We take our tea in Tartary, or chop at Coromandel.
1939J. Cary Mister Johnson (1952) 213: Chop all right?
1962R. Brathwait Kind of Homecoming 121: I\xe2\x80\x99ve some friends there and we can be sure of a bed and some chop.
1977(con. 1969) M. Herr Dispatches 7: Three-star war food, the same chop they sold at Abercrombie & Fitch.
1984J. Carson in Ammon Working Lives 146: He told me to \xe2\x80\x98get in for my chop.\xe2\x80\x99 That was no trouble at all as I was growing fast and could eat like a horse.

2. a boat-load of tea.

1864, 1867, 1870Hotten Sl. Dict.
1873Sl. Dict.

3. (US Und.) tobacco.

1909F.H. Tillotson How I Became a Detective 90: Chop \xe2\x80\x93 Tobacco.
