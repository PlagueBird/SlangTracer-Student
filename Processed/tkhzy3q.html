chuck v.1

[Grose (1796) suggests that the term applies to women only]

1. to have sexual intercourse.

c.1550A. Scott Poems (1821) 25: Fairwell with chestetie, ffra wechis fall to chucking.
1638R. Brathwait Barnabees Journal II I6: Thence at Meredin [...] I repos\xe2\x80\x99d, where I chuck\xe2\x80\x99t Jone-a.
1659Greene & Lodge Lady Alimony V vi: Ile rather go mumble a crust at home: and chuck my old Josalin.
1664C. Cotton Virgil Travestie (1765) Bk I 30: And let herself be chuck\xe2\x80\x99d / [...] / By Mars, and many a one beside.

2. (later use is UK black) of a woman, to make sexual advances [note cluck v.1 , for which this may be a mis-reading].

1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue n.p.: Chuck, to shew a propensity for a man. The mort chucks; the wench wants to be doing.
1811Lex. Balatronicum.
1823Egan Grose\xe2\x80\x99s Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1997C. Newland Scholar 33: What, you chuckin\xe2\x80\x99 it wid my girl spee, or what?
