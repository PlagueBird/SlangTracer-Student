pull n.

1. as a power over someone/something.

(a) influence, advantage.

c.1500Medwall Interlude of Nature sig. C. ii: It cost me a noble... The scald capper sware, That yt cost hym euen as myche But there Pryde had a pull [F&H].
1781J. Burgoyne Lord of Manor III i: You\xe2\x80\x99ll have quite the pull of me in employment.
a.1790H.T. Potter New Dict. Cant (1795) n.p.: pull having the advantage over another.
c.1800 \xe2\x80\x98Drunk in the Night\xe2\x80\x99 No. 26 Papers of Francis Place (1819) n.p.: It was my intention, to have a pull on them without more delay / So without further trouble I tip\xe2\x80\x99d them the double.
1812Vaux Vocab. of the Flash Lang. in McLachlan (1964) 259: pull An important advantage possessed by one party over another; as in gaming, you may by some slight, unknown to your adversary, or by a knowledge of the cards, &c., have the odds of winning considerably on your side; you are then said to have a great pull. To have the power of injuring a person, by the knowledge of any thing erroneous in his conduct, which leaves his character or personal safety at your mercy, is also termed having a pull upon him, that is (to use a vulgar phrase) that you have him under your thumb.
1821Egan Life in London (1869) 227: [The Watchmen] besides having the pull in their favour, in opening the charge, and colouring it as they think proper .
1828\xe2\x80\x98A Flat Enlightened\xe2\x80\x99 Life in the West I 152: [of card-playing] \xe2\x80\x98The fairest game played, on which there can be no pull, is this\xe2\x80\x99 [etc].

1842Whip & Satirist of NY & Brooklyn (NY) 14 May n.p.: Main science being the only pull at the game [i.e. billiards].

1905H. Green Actors\xe2\x80\x99 Boarding House (1906) 27: Well, yuh gotta have pull tuh do it.

(b) a trick, a fraud, a knack.

1608Dekker Belman of London F2: Diuerse other pullies (if these two faile) haue they to draw simple men into their company.
1793Sporting Mag. July II 234/2: Mr. Lookup won between three and four hundred pounds; but it having been hinted to Sir Thomas [...] that Lookup must have had a pull upon him, the baronet commenced an action to recover double damages.
1812Vaux Vocab. of the Flash Lang. in McLachlan (1964) 259: pull [...] A person speaking of any intricate affair, or feat of ingenuity, which he cannot comprehend, will say, There is some pull at the bottom of it, that I\xe2\x80\x99m not fly to.
c.1880in Matthews Cockney Past and Present (1938) 57: A werry tidy pull for coves with a bit of money to lay out.

(c) (US) an unacceptable act or situation.

1834Ely\xe2\x80\x99s Hawk & Buzzard (NY) Mar. 22 2/2: He used to [...] fall in with his blossom, who would make no bones to introduce him to the larder [...] Now this is a dead pull, and there must be a stop put to it.

(d) a physical advantage.

1853G.J. Whyte-Melville Digby Grand (1890) 59: You are the lightest weight, a great pull on snow-shoes.
1864J.F. Mortlock Experiences of a Convict (1965) 26: At roulette the \xe2\x80\x98pull\xe2\x80\x99 in favour of the table was about nineteen to eighteen.

(e) an ulterior motive, a hidden agenda.

1890 \xe2\x80\x98\xe2\x80\x99Arry on the \xe2\x80\x99Oliday Season\xe2\x80\x99 in Punch 16 Aug. 74/1: Life\xe2\x80\x99s greatest pulls, dontcherknow / Are to look up to sparklers above us, and down on poor duffers below.
1896Ade Artie (1963) 30: \xe2\x80\x98Come off,\xe2\x80\x99 I says; \xe2\x80\x98he would n\xe2\x80\x99t be writin\xe2\x80\x99 notes and comin\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x99round here unless he had some pull.\xe2\x80\x99.

(f) an anxious or worrying moment that \xe2\x80\x98tugs at one\xe2\x80\x99s heartstrings\xe2\x80\x99.

1898Eve. Star (Wash., DC) 24 Dec. 1/5: I [...] takes another swingin\xe2\x80\x99 big pull at the rum.

\n In phrases\n

get the pull on \n (v.)\n

to have at a disadvantage.

1915J. Buchan Thirty-Nine Steps (1930) 74: It made me boil with rage to think of those three spies getting the pull on me like this.
give someone a pull \n (v.)\n

1. to tell off, to reprimand.

1958F. Norman Bang To Rights 23: Spud Murphy gave him a very strong pull, and put the frighteners on him.

2. to arrest.

1965F. Norman in Punch 17 Mar. in Norman\xe2\x80\x99s London (1969) 158: One [policeman] even gave me a pull and told me that I was very naughty not to go around committing crimes.

3. to notify.

2010K. Richards Life 530: I got a pull from Chrissy Kingston [...] about this amazing mongrel.
