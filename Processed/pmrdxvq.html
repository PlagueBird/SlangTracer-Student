tip n.2

[SE tip, a gratuity]

1. money as used in any form of contract.

1812Vaux Vocab. of the Flash Lang. in McLachlan (1964) 274: The tip is a term frequently used to signify the money concerned in any dealings or contract existing between parties; synonymous with the dues.
1821\xe2\x80\x98An Amateur\xe2\x80\x99 Real Life in London I 145: I knows very well as how he\xe2\x80\x99s got the tip in his pocket.
1830Egan Finish to the Adventures of Tom and Jerry (1889) 119: All I looks after is the tip \xe2\x80\x93 and to prevent bolters.
1833\xe2\x80\x98Flare Up!\xe2\x80\x99 in Rake\xe2\x80\x99s Budget in Spedding & Watt (eds) Bawdy Songbooks (2011) III 66: Eere they commenc\xe2\x80\x99d \xe2\x80\x94 to stash all frauds, / She wanted tip.
1848Flash Dict. in Sinks of London Laid Open.
1861(con. 1840s\xe2\x80\x9350s) H. Mayhew London Labour and London Poor I 425/1: The party of the house produces the bill, which promises a stunning tip for the old lumber.
1870Hotten Sl. Dict.
1880E.J. Milliken \xe2\x80\x98Cad\xe2\x80\x99s Calendar\xe2\x80\x99 in Punch Almanack n.p.: The Boss will drop a tip, \xe2\x80\x93 \xe2\x80\x99ope so, at least.

2. money.

1866Letters by an Odd Boy 101: \xe2\x80\x98Look here, got any tip?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Tip?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Ugh! yer six days old! Eyes not open yet, eh! \xe2\x80\x94 blunt, stumpy, coppers \xe2\x80\x94 a joey \xe2\x80\x94 a tanner \xe2\x80\x94 a bob \xe2\x80\x94 money, yer muff\xe2\x80\x99.

3. a bribe.

1812Vaux Vocab. of the Flash Lang. in McLachlan (1964) 274: tip: to give, pay, or bribe. To take the tip, is to receive a bribe in any shape; and they say of a person who is known to be corruptible, that he will stand the tip.
1852Bathurst Free Press (NSW) 21 Apr. 2/2: Now the slang phraseology of New South Wales contains such a word as \xe2\x80\x98tip,\xe2\x80\x99 the elegant! English of which is \xe2\x80\x98bribery\xe2\x80\x99.
1882Nat. Police Gaz. (NY) 18 Mar. 2/1: [I]n sporting events, all the fellows who have taken \xe2\x80\x98tips\xe2\x80\x99 have been turned over.

\n In compounds\n

tip street \n (n.)\n

a state of wealth; thus in tip street, well-off.

1821Egan Life in London (1869) 217: Jerry is in Tip Street upon this occasion and the Mollishers are all nutty upon him.
1835G. Kent Modern Flash Dict. 33: Tip street, to be in \xe2\x80\x93 to have plenty of money.
1848Flash Dict. in Sinks of London Laid Open [as cit. 1835].
