canary n.1

[synon. with bird n.1 , a woman, underlined by the gaudy colouring of her clothes and/or SE canary, a small fluttering bird]

1. senses pertaining to women.

(a) the vagina.

1691 \xe2\x80\x98The Vindication of Top-Knots and Commodes\xe2\x80\x99 in Ebsworth Bagford Ballads (1878) I 123: Then silly old Fops, that kiss but like popes, / And call us Night Walkers and Faries, / Go fumble old Joan, and let us alone, / And never come near our canary\xe2\x80\x99s.

(b) (also canary-bird) a mistress; a girlfriend.

1681Otway Soldier\xe2\x80\x99s Fortune IV ii: Come hither, hussy, you little canary-bird.
1914Sport (Adelaide) 21 Feb. 10/1: They Say [...] That Archie K. has got a new cage to put his little canary, Miss A., in.

(c) a prostitute.

1676Etherege Man of Mode I i: Three quarts of canary is her business.
1826Pierce Egan\xe2\x80\x99s Life in London 19 Nov. 765/2: [H]is admirers are rather on the fret, saying, \xe2\x80\x98as how, the Gas lately has been too fond of the Canary Birds; and that he is none the better for it.
1842Whip & Satirist of NY & Brooklyn (NY) 23 Apr. n.p.: the whip wants to know [...] Why the Man-a-warsman did not catch the canary bird and put her under his wing.
1989H. Rawson Dict. of Invective (1991) 71: canary. A woman, sometimes a whore.

(d) (also canary-bird) a thief\xe2\x80\x99s female accomplice.

1861(con. 1840s\xe2\x80\x9350s) H. Mayhew London Labour and London Poor IV 337/2: Sometimes a woman, called a \xe2\x80\x98canary,\xe2\x80\x99 carries the tools, and watches outside.
1899A.C. Gunter M.S. Bradford Special 171: With this charming canary bird under my thumb.
1935A.J. Pollock Und. Speaks 18/1: Canary, a woman lookout or spotter for thieves.
1940J. Archibald \xe2\x80\x98No Place Like Homicide\xe2\x80\x99 in Popular Detective Apr. I am desperate, and my canary is waiting over at the tavern.

(e) (US) a woman.

1886N.O. Lantern n.p.: He acquired the art of making a canary understand that he wished to meet her on the corner at night by merely making a motion with his hands [R].
1901\xe2\x80\x98Hugh McHugh\xe2\x80\x99 John Henry 93: The old canary was still hunting me up with eyes ablaze with love.
1902Ade Girl Proposition 141: He put his Tag on a blonde Canary 17 Years of Age.
1938Eve. Post (Wellington) 25 Jan. 8/8: Modern Americanisms [...] The names for girls are legion [...] \xe2\x80\x98Canary,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Hairpin,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Sardine,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Hotsie-Totsie\xe2\x80\x99 or plain \xe2\x80\x99darb\xe2\x80\x99.
1944P. Kendall Dict. Service Sl. n.p.: a canary . . . a beautiful, glamorous girl.
1949L. Glassop Lucky Palmer 41: \xe2\x80\x98Charlie?\xe2\x80\x99 asked Eric. \xe2\x80\x98What do you mean by “Charlie?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Your “Charlie,\xe2\x80\x99 repeated Max. \xe2\x80\x98Your canary.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98“Canary?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Ay, don\xe2\x80\x99t you speak English? Your sheila.\xe2\x80\x99.
1989 see sense 1c above.
2001 cwnews \xe2\x80\x98Events\xe2\x80\x99 in Eye on CameraWare III Apr. There were skirts of every variety: tall drinks of water, gals with great gams, blond canaries, sweet sisters with sultry intent.
2017T. Pluck Bad Boy Boogie [ebook] \xe2\x80\x98She sure is one hot canary\xe2\x80\x99.

(f) (US campus) a female student at a mixed college.

1900E.H. Babbitt \xe2\x80\x98College Words and Phrases\xe2\x80\x99 in DN II:i 26: canary, n. A woman student at a co-educational college.

2. senses based on the yellow colour of the bird.

(a) (orig. Aus.) a convict [the yellow uniforms that they wore and their being \xe2\x80\x98caged\xe2\x80\x99; poss. reinforced by earlier canary-bird n.1 (2) although that image refers to the cage rather than the colour].

1827P. Cunningham New South Wales II 117: Convicts of but recent migration are facetiously known by the name of canaries, by reason of the yellow plumage in which they are fledged at the period of landing.
1852Launceston Examiner (Tas.) 24 Dec. 862/3: [I]t is their wish they should be regarded as \xe2\x80\x98transports,\xe2\x80\x99 and let society comply with the request. Let them be known hereafter as Canaries, Yellow-Greys, or \xe2\x80\x98our people,\xe2\x80\x99 as the public choose.
1890\xe2\x80\x98Rolf Boldrewood\xe2\x80\x99 Colonial Reformer I 78: Can\xe2\x80\x99t you get your canaries off the track here for about a quarter of an hour, and let my mob of cattle pass?
1906Sun. Times (Perth) 18 Nov. 1/1: G.L. was one day snipe shooting at Rottnest. Came across a convict [...] \xe2\x80\x98Who sent you here?\xe2\x80\x99 asked Leake. \xe2\x80\x98You,\xe2\x80\x99 replied the canary.
1933Ersine Und. and Prison Sl.
1945Baker Aus. Lang. 41: A large number of synonyms for convict became current, among them canary, transport, old hand, crossbred.
1949Monteleone Criminal Sl. (rev. edn).
1950Goldin et al. DAUL 39/2: Canary. 1. (Rare) A convict.
1967P. Thomas Down These Mean Streets (1970) 259: Sometimes the pressure got too much to bear, as it does with all cons, who, seeking a release from the overpowering hatred against a society that makes canaries out of human beings, let out their aggressions on each other.
1971J. McClure Steam Pig 102: Constable! Take those two canaries and put them in separate cells.

(b) a yellow silk handkerchief, as worn by a costermonger, allegedly popularized by the British prize-fighter John Gully (1783\xe2\x80\x931863).

1823\xe2\x80\x98Jon Bee\xe2\x80\x99 Dict. of the Turf, the Ring, the Chase, etc. 87: None can be a gilliflower who does not wear a canary or belcher fogle round his twist.

(c) a guinea; a sovereign; a gold coin.

1840Courier (Hobart, Tas.) 27 Oct. 3/1: [advert, from UK source] [A] pair of Kicksies, built very slap, with the artful dodge, a Canary and 2 bulls.
1860Hotten Dict. of Modern Sl. etc. (2nd edn).
1864, 1867, 1870Hotten Sl. Dict.
1873Sl. Dict.
1885Bulletin (Sydney) 3 Oct. 14/3: [T]he cost attaching to the operation is only a modest \xe2\x80\x98canary\xe2\x80\x99.
1925Western Mail (Perth) 28 May 21/1: [from Daily Mail, London] A sovereign had a lot of slang names [...] a portrait, a yellow boy, [...] a canary, a couter, a foont, a poona [and] a bean.
1937Nott. Eve. Post 30 Apr. 6/3: Lesser known nicknames for sovereigns [...] \xe2\x80\x98chip\xe2\x80\x99 [...] \xe2\x80\x98canary,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98nob,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98old Mr Gory\xe2\x80\x99 [...] and \xe2\x80\x98shiner\xe2\x80\x99.

(d) a half-sovereign (50p).

1895Western Champion (Barcaldine) 31 Dec. 9/5: A profitable profession it seemed, too, judging from the cool way they talked of \xe2\x80\x98John Dunns\xe2\x80\x99 (£1), \xe2\x80\x98thick \xe2\x80\x99uns\xe2\x80\x99 (sovs.), \xe2\x80\x98canarys\xe2\x80\x99 (halfsovs.), \xe2\x80\x98finn\xe2\x80\x99 (£5), &c [AND].

(e) (UK prison) a prisoner who has been caught in an escape attempt.

1874J. Greenwood Wilds of London (1881) 53: Amongst the gangs in the quarries may be seen here and there a convict with one leg yellow and the other of a cinnamon colour, and the same as to his arms and each half of his body [...] These are known as \xe2\x80\x98canaries,\xe2\x80\x99 and are convicts who have attempted to escape.

(f) a convict\xe2\x80\x99s yellow jacket.

1874M. Clarke Term of His Natural Life (1897) 342: We can\xe2\x80\x99t bring him off, if it is him, in his canaries.

(g) (US) a mule; usu. in comb. with a geographical name, e.g. Rocky Mountain canary (bird) n.

1876\xe2\x80\x98Dan de Quille\xe2\x80\x99 Big Bonanza (1947) 73: The donkey, called by everybody in that region, \xe2\x80\x98The Washoe Canary\xe2\x80\x99.
1896A. Morrison Child of the Jago (1982) 60: Jerry Gullen\xe2\x80\x99s canary was no bird, but a donkey.
1909 (ref. to 1882) in J. Ware Passing Eng. of the Victorian Era.

(h) a form on which one signs a promise to make a donation to the Salvation Army; a charity subscription [the yellow paper used by the Salvation Army, whose colours were red and yellow (the red paper was more expensive, noted Ware); coined by their founder William Booth (1829\xe2\x80\x931912)].

1887 \xe2\x80\x98\xe2\x80\x99Arry on the Jubilee\xe2\x80\x99 in Punch 25 June 305/1: We\xe2\x80\x99ll provide them with L.S.D., Charlie, [...] But if we supply the canaries, the toppers must let us \xe2\x80\x99ave larks.

(i) (Aus./N.Z., also canary-face) a Chinese immigrant [yellow adj. (2c)].

1898Bulletin (Sydney) 1 Oct. 14/3: A few more W. Q. slang words. . . Tobacco is \xe2\x80\x98snout\xe2\x80\x99, opium \xe2\x80\x98twang\xe2\x80\x99, a Chinaman a \xe2\x80\x98canary\xe2\x80\x99, and a blackfellow is a \xe2\x80\x98swatser\xe2\x80\x99.
1912R.S. Tait Scotty Mac 42: What\xe2\x80\x99s the matter, canary face? [AND].
1971Bulletin (Sydney) 18 Sept. 47: Australian attitudes towards the almond-eyed celestials, the chinks, chows, pongs, canaries and dinks which have so resolutely clung to the fringes of the national consciousness.

(j) (US campus) a cigarette, presumably in a yellow packet or yellow paper.

1900E.H. Babbitt \xe2\x80\x98College Words and Phrases\xe2\x80\x99 in DN II:i 26: canary, n. A cigarette.

(k) (US black) a mulatto girl [yellow adj. (2b)].

1944 \xe2\x80\x98Jiver\xe2\x80\x99s Bible\xe2\x80\x99 in D. Burley Orig. Hbk of Harlem Jive.

(l) (Irish) a fright, i.e. one turns yellow adj. (4)

1981(con. 1920s) P. Crosbie Your Dinner\xe2\x80\x99s Poured Out! 219: I nearly had a canary (with fright or shock).
2002O\xe2\x80\x99Byrne Files: Dublin Sl. Dict. Canary, got a phr. n. Fright.

3. (Aus.) 100 strokes of the lash [fig. use of sense 2c above: the term plays on the monetary value signifying the number of strokes].

1788in Sun (Sydney) 16 Apr. (1912) 9/2: Sydney in 1788 [...] He failed to locate the mine, even at the bayonet\xe2\x80\x99s point, and received a canary , or one hundred lashes, to be followed by an other hundred, and yet another hundred, until a gold mine materialised. The canary made Peter whistle.
1853J. Lang Botany Bay 40: There were slang terms applied to these doses of the lash; twenty-five was called a \xe2\x80\x98tester;\xe2\x80\x99 fifty \xe2\x80\x98a bob;\xe2\x80\x99 seventy five \xe2\x80\x98a bull\xe2\x80\x99 and a hundred a \xe2\x80\x98canary\xe2\x80\x99.
1901(con. early 19C) Sydney Sportsman (Surry Hills, NSW) 20 Feb. 2/7: Hughes cursed him so very violently that he attracted the attention of one of the overseers, who promptly told him that \xe2\x80\x98gallus or no gallus, he\xe2\x80\x99d get half a canary (i.e., 50 lashes) if he didn\xe2\x80\x99t keep quiet\xe2\x80\x99.
1906(con. early 19C) Cobargo Chron. (NSW) 2 Nov. 4/2: [as 1853].
1945(con. 1802) Baker Aus. Lang. 44: Nor, though they are formed on English slang terms for coins, are [recorded] [...] canary, one hundred lashes.

4. based on the bird\xe2\x80\x99s singing.

(a) (US) a chorus-singer placed in the gallery from where they urge on the rest of the audience.

1886Referee Mar. in Ware (1909) 62/1: Chorus-singing by the canaries has long been a South London Institution.
1909J. Ware Passing Eng. of the Victorian Era 62/1: Canary (Music Hall, 1870). Chorus-singer amongst the public \xe2\x80\x93 generally in gallery. Invented by Leybourne, a comic singer, probably to give him rest between his verses, he being pulmonary. \xe2\x80\x98Go it, canaries\xe2\x80\x99, he flatteringly would say.

(b) (show business) a female singer, usu. fronting a band.

1901\xe2\x80\x98Hugh McHugh\xe2\x80\x99 Down the Line 38: Across the aisle sat two pet canaries from Plainfield, New Jersey. They were [...] en route to the West to join the \xe2\x80\x98Bunch of Birds Burlesque Company\xe2\x80\x99.
1909J. Ware Passing Eng. of the Victorian Era.
1937R.B. Nye \xe2\x80\x98A Musician\xe2\x80\x99s Word List\xe2\x80\x99 in AS XII:1 45: canary. A woman vocalist.
1938W. Winchell On Broadway 11 Mar. [synd. col.] Helen Jepson being pressured by the autografools, now that she is a Sam Goldwyn star intead of just a Metropolitan Opera canary.
1942\xe2\x80\x98Marienne\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Solid Meddlin\xe2\x80\x99\xe2\x80\x99 in People\xe2\x80\x99s Voice (NY) 14 Mar. 33/1: That ace canary, Ella Fitzgerald.
1953L. Durst Jives of Dr. Hepcat (1989) 5: Some frantic canary will go into her act with the latest issue.
1956Girl Can\xe2\x80\x99t Help It [film script] You once had a great nose for finding new talent. Dug up some big canaries, but the booze got in your way.
1960, 1975Wentworth & Flexner DAS.
1975L. Rosten Dear \xe2\x80\x98Herm\xe2\x80\x99 9: \xe2\x80\x98My cousin “Leon does not live in New York!\xe2\x80\x99 that crazy canary confides.
1989H. Rawson Dict. of Invective (1991) 71: canary. A woman, [...] frequently but not necessarily a singer, a.k.a. chirp or thrush.
2002J. Ridley Conversation with the Mann 91: The kitten\xe2\x80\x99s a canary. She something, isn\xe2\x80\x99t she?

(c) (UK/US/S.Afr. Und.) an informer.

1929Hostetter & Beesley It\xe2\x80\x99s a Racket! 221: canary \xe2\x80\x94 Informer; especially, one who gives information to the police.
1939Howsley Argot: Dict. of Und. Sl.
1946N.Y. Times 15 Dec. SM16: Singer: an informer [...] also canary.
1952I, Mobster 95: They\xe2\x80\x99ve got plenty of trained canaries to sing any way they tell them.
1955B. Schulberg On the Waterfront (1964) 258: Is he D \xe2\x80\x99n D or is he a canary?
1962Ragen & Finston World\xe2\x80\x99s Toughest Prison 793: canary \xe2\x80\x93 An informer.
1976Sutton & Linn Where The Money Was (2004) 350: The Bronx County jail, more popularly known as the Singing School because that\xe2\x80\x99s where they always sent the canaries.
1989H. Rawson Dict. of Invective (1991) 71: In the underworld, a canary is another kind of \xe2\x80\x98singer,\xe2\x80\x99 i.e., an informer, a.k.a. nightingale, pigeon or rat.
1990Tupper & Wortley Aus. Prison Sl. Gloss. Canary. An informer. From \xe2\x80\x98to sing like a canary\xe2\x80\x99.
1994I. Welsh \xe2\x80\x98Stoke Newington Blues\xe2\x80\x99 in Acid House 37: We got the world\xe2\x80\x99s first black canary chirping away twenty to the dozen next door.
2004T. Dorsey Cadillac Beach 258: \xe2\x80\x98What jumps into your head?\xe2\x80\x99 [...] \xe2\x80\x98Chicago overcoats, Harlem sunsets, a jorum of skee, [...] giving a canary the Broderick . . .\xe2\x80\x99.
2010Codella and Bennett Alphaville (2011) 363: Whjat we learned from the thirty-nine canaries we caged was that [...] he had indeed fallen out with Animal and Guerro.

(d) (US Und.) a loquacious person, a chatterer.

1933C.G. Booth \xe2\x80\x98Stag Party\xe2\x80\x99 in Penzler Pulp Fiction (2006) 121: \xe2\x80\x98A swell dish you canaries handed me last night,\xe2\x80\x99 the house manager said sourly.
1935A.J. Pollock Und. Speaks n.p.: Canary, a talkative person.

(e) a singer, irrespective of gender; also attrib.

1938Pittsburgh Courier (PA) 30 July 11/1: The ace canary of swing trucked to the P.A.
1939 \xe2\x80\x98Citadel Gloss.\xe2\x80\x99 in AS XIV:1 Feb. 25/2: canary club, n. 1. The cadet glee club. 2. The cadet choir.
1959C. MacInnes Absolute Beginners 110: Zesty-Boy [...] started writing for the top pop canaries, and made piles.

1919V. Marshall World of Living Dead (1969) 101: The heat of midsummer lay stagnant in the windowless \xe2\x80\x98canary cage\xe2\x80\x99 compartment of the prison van.

have a hairy canary \n (v.)\n

(US) to have a temper tantrum, an emotional outburst.

1984Partridge DSUE (8th edn) 772: [...] since ca. 1955.
1987Larry King Live [CNN-TV] If the Russians put a strategic force in Canada or Mexico we\xe2\x80\x99d have a hairy canary [HDAS].
2012V. Jarrett Nothing Is Heavy [ebook] \xe2\x80\x98My boss is having a hairy canary that you don't want to check it out before you hand over your cash\xe2\x80\x99.
2018T. Tappan Moon-Riders [ebook] Breen having a hairy canary every five minutes was rapidly losing its charm.
have the canaries (in one\xe2\x80\x99s head) \n (v.)\n

1. (US) to be mentally unstable; for one\xe2\x80\x99s brain to be injured.

1947B. Schulberg Harder They Fall (1971) 287: I had a strong hunch that Gus had the canaries in his head after Stein.

2. (Irish) to have an emotional outburst.
