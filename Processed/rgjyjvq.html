cop n.1

[note Cumbrian dial. cop, a prison]

1. (orig. US, a police officer, thus N.Z.) cop on wheels, a traffic officer.

1859 \xe2\x80\x98Hundred Stretches Hence\xe2\x80\x99 in Matsell Vocabulum 124: And where the buffer, bruiser, blowen, / And all the cops and beaks so knowin\xe2\x80\x99.
1865Leaves from Diary of Celebrated Burglar 5/2: This [fight] did not last long, as the \xe2\x80\x98cops\xe2\x80\x99 soon forced their way in.
1873 \xe2\x80\x98The Street Arabs of New York\xe2\x80\x99 in Appleton\xe2\x80\x99s Journal (N.Y.) 4 Jan. 47: And then as to the cops \xe2\x80\x94 they can\xe2\x80\x99t catch the brats.
1874Cairo Bull. (Cairo, IL) 5 Nov. 2/3: [from The Graphic, London] Let Blowens flash their ivories and toast the cops to-night.
1881Bulletin (Sydney) 19 Feb. 8/4: She served him with the best of brands, / From bottles with glass stoppers, / When \xe2\x80\x93 like weak mortals \xe2\x80\x93 of a morn / That brave \xe2\x80\x98cop\xe2\x80\x99 had \xe2\x80\x98hot coppers.\xe2\x80\x99.
1882Sydney Sl. Dict. (2 edn) 9: Dick went pulling down sawney for grub last week, when a cop pinched him. / Dick went stealing bacon from shop-doors for food last week, when a policeman arrested him.
1887G. Devol Forty Years a Gambler 53: We [...] lit out, for fear that the little old silver man would bring back a \xe2\x80\x98cop\xe2\x80\x99.
1890\xe2\x80\x98F. Anstey\xe2\x80\x99 Voces Populi 40: Them cowards of cops \xe2\x80\x99ave as much on their \xe2\x80\x99ands as they kin do with.
1890 (ref. to 1847) G.W. Walling Recollections 38: One of the men said to the others, \xe2\x80\x98Get up \xe2\x80\x93 the “cops are here.\xe2\x80\x99.
1904Sun. Times (Perth) 13 Mar. 5/3: [headline] A Cop\xe2\x80\x99s Career.
1905Marvel III:53 5: Watch them cops dust the road wiv him fer his bloomin\xe2\x80\x99 cheek!
1911J. Masefield Everlasting Mercy 55: I staggered into street again / With mind made up (or primed with gin) / To bash the cop who\xe2\x80\x99d run me in.
1912N.Z. Truth 13 Jan. 8/2: The \xe2\x80\x98cop\xe2\x80\x99 [...] took him to the lock-up.
1922E.E. Cummings Enormous Room (1928) 33: The Head Cop has particularly requested the pleasure of this distinguished American\xe2\x80\x99s company at déjeuner.
1930(con. 1900s\xe2\x80\x9310s) Dos Passos 42nd Parallel in USA (1966) 63: Jeez, I thought you were a cop.
1932(con. 1919) Dos Passos Nineteen Nineteen in USA (1966) 354: He was a striker parading beaten up by the cops.
1935H. McCoy They Shoot Horses, Don\xe2\x80\x99t They? in Four Novels (1983) 9: I decided to [...] make the cops take care of me.
1946Mezzrow & Wolfe Really the Blues 87: We already had the cops out looking for him.
1953\xe2\x80\x98William Lee\xe2\x80\x99 Junkie (1966) 23: Any cop would do a double-take at the sight of him.
1957C. Himes Imabelle 50: A young white cop had arrested a middle-aged drunken coloured woman.
1965C. Himes Rage in Harlem (1969) 51: [as 1957].
1974(con. 1960s) R. Price Wanderers 16: I was gonna call the cops in ten minutes.
1984B. Ellem Doing Time 189: cop: a policeman.
1989C. Hiaasen Skin Tight 14: The only Tim he knew was an ex-homicide cop named Gavigan.
1998Source Oct. 178: Are you afraid of the cops finding out?
2000Observer Mag. 9 Jan. 15: \xe2\x80\x98What happened?\xe2\x80\x99 he asked a cop.
2001D. Looser Boobslang [U. Canterbury D.Phil. thesis] 46/1: cop on wheels n. a traffic officer.

2. (orig. US) an arrest; esp. in the old (and prob. fictional) cliché, It\xe2\x80\x99s a fair cop, guv, slap the bracelets on.

1891J. Bent Criminal Life 67: As soon as I found the watch, the thief called out, \xe2\x80\x98This is a clear cop, Mr. Bent\xe2\x80\x99.
1896A. Morrison Child of the Jago (1982) 192: Awright [...] I\xe2\x80\x99m done; it\xe2\x80\x99s a cop.
1923Nottingham Eve. Post 17 Jan. 5/4: When Cox was questioned he said \xe2\x80\x98It is a clan cop. I broke in\xe2\x80\x99.
1930\xe2\x80\x98Leslie Charteris\xe2\x80\x99 Enter the Saint 133: Tell me, Spider [...] is this or is this not entitled to be called a cop?
1948C. Day Lewis Otterbury Incident 167: \xe2\x80\x98Stick \xe2\x80\x99em up, the lot of you!\xe2\x80\x99 he said. \xe2\x80\x98It\xe2\x80\x99s a cop!\xe2\x80\x99.
1958(con. 1940s) B. Behan Borstal Boy 285: You were probably the first cop they\xe2\x80\x99d made. [Ibid.] 341: \xe2\x80\x98It\xe2\x80\x99s a fair cop,\xe2\x80\x99 said Joe.
2000Sun. Times Mag. 12 Mar. 18: Am I supposed to get parts saying, \xe2\x80\x98Yes, guv, it\xe2\x80\x99s a fair cop, you\xe2\x80\x99ve got me bang to rights?\xe2\x80\x99.

3. (UK prison) an inmate.

1925N. Lucas Autobiog. of a Thief 92: I heard the cry : \xe2\x80\x98New cop! New cop!\xe2\x80\x99 taken up and repeated in shrill Cockney voices.

4. a sentence.

1937R. Westerby Wide Boys Never Work (1938) 183: Seven years. That\xe2\x80\x99s the cop.

5. (US prison) a warder, a guard.

1976Edwardsville Intelligencer (IL) 30 Mar. 2/2: Guards [...] are called \xe2\x80\x98cops\xe2\x80\x99 in federal institutions and \xe2\x80\x98bulls\xe2\x80\x99 in state facilities.
1985Southern Illinoisan (Carbondale, IL) 7 Apr. 4/1: Prison Slang [...] Police, hack, pig, cop. Guards.
1997T. Fontana \xe2\x80\x98Straight Life\xe2\x80\x99 Oz ser. 1 ep. 5 [TV script] Kitchen\xe2\x80\x99s the first place the cops would look [i,.e for drug smuggling].
2002J. Lerner You Got Nothing Coming 59: If he didn\xe2\x80\x99t want the cops to spot his face at the wire-reinforced cell window, he would drop to the floor and shout under the door.
2010A. Steinberg Running the Books 167: How you named those people made you either a con or a cop. There was [...] no neutrality.

cop-caller \n (n.)\n

(US black) one who, when subjected to crime, reports it and cimplains rather than keeps silent.

1971R.D. Pharr S.R.O. (1998) 47: \xe2\x80\x98Some pussies are cop-callers [...] Jews are notorious cop-callers. And they are a determined lot\xe2\x80\x99.
cop house \n (n.)\n

(US) a police station.

1928C. Coe Swag 57: Just what did they ask you over there at the cop house?
1958R. Chandler Playback 165: The cop house was part of a long, modernistic building.
1970J. Conaway Big Easy 137: This is your man at the cop house [...] I\xe2\x80\x99m quitting.
1977Lou Grant 20 Sept. [TV series] [episode title] Cophouse.
1996 \xe2\x80\x98Lance Lawson\xe2\x80\x99 at www.lileks.com The prisoner looked around, confused. \xe2\x80\x98Wha \xe2\x80\x93 this isn\xe2\x80\x99t the cophouse. This is a parking lot for a hash joint.\xe2\x80\x99.
2005R. Wiley ESPN Let me just say this, just to let you in on how it goes in the phone room on the night cophouse beat.
cop killer \n (n.)\n

(US) a Teflon-coated bullet capable of penetrating the body armour worn by police officers; such bullets are outlawed.

1982 Cop Killer Bullets [NBC-TV programme] .
1996Minnesota Daily Online 16 May President Clinton memorialized slain police officers Wednesday as \xe2\x80\x98America\xe2\x80\x99s heroes\xe2\x80\x99 and urged Congress to ban \xe2\x80\x98cop-killer\xe2\x80\x99 bullets so officers can feel safer.

cop shop \n (n.)\n [shop n.1 (1)]

(orig. Aus.) a police station; cite 2008 refers to a city\xe2\x80\x99s police department.

1917Mirror (Sydney) 13 Oct. 3: A lump on his fat head as big as an egg, thanks to the tripehound\xe2\x80\x99s rudder work, And while he was in dreamland, Spotty dragged him hence, and he\xe2\x80\x99ll rest in the cop-shop while his bump goes down, and a bit longer, we hope.
1928Gundagai Times (NSW) 13 JAn. 3/2: Taking them to the cop-shop in the car, he charged them.
1931Truth (Sydney) 21 June 12/5: The policeman promptly steered for the nearest cop-shop \xe2\x80\x94 with George In tow.
1944I. Holden There\xe2\x80\x99s No Story There 89: Outside the cop-shop two policemen were staring after him.
1946Cootamundra Herald (NSW) 19 Aug. 2/1: \xe2\x80\x98Look, lady, this is the cop shop, not an S.P. shop,\xe2\x80\x99 broke in Sgt Loomes.
1958(con. 1940s) B. Behan Borstal Boy 285: He brought me to the cop-shop.
1969A. Buzo Rooted I iii: They put him in a paddy wagon with all the pros and cons. We had to go up to the cop shop and bail him out.
1971N. Smith Gumshoe (1998) 174: \xe2\x80\x98Where\xe2\x80\x99s the money, lad?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Down the cop shop.\xe2\x80\x99.
1986R.G. Barrett Real Thing 61: I\xe2\x80\x99ve been past the cop-shop three bloody times.
1992G. Disher Paydirt [ebook] The nearest cop shop is an hour away. I never saw a single jack .
1998I. Welsh Filth 274: One part of the bar is full of polis from the Leith cop shop.
2001A. Sillitoe Birthday 117: They\xe2\x80\x99ve got my mug in a thousand copshops.
2004J. Ellroy \xe2\x80\x98Hot-Prowl Rape-O\xe2\x80\x99 in Destination: Morgue! (2004) 294: We hit the cop shop. Cops recognized Donna.
2008Simon & Pelecanos \xe2\x80\x98Late Editions\xe2\x80\x99 Wire ser. 5 ep. 9 [TV script] What about this Daniels. You think he\xe2\x80\x99s ready to run the cop shop.
2009P. Temple Truth 16: There\xe2\x80\x99s like a whole suburb of unsold million-buck apartments. All spruiked to be as safe as living next door to the Benalla copshop in 1952.
2012 G. Johnstone \xe2\x80\x98No Through Road\xe2\x80\x99 in Crime Factory: Hard Labour [ebook] A few hours later I\xe2\x80\x99m sat in a cop shop on St Kilda Road.
2021P. Papathanasiou Stoning 13: The cop shop phone rang out.

\n In phrases\n

cop sling \n (v.)\n

(Aus. und.) to pass on one\xe2\x80\x99s booty to an associate, either voluntarily or prior to a possible/actual arrest.

1882Sydney Sl. Dict. (2 edn) 2: Cop Bung (or Sling) - To hand over plunder to another to prevent its being found if the thief be given in charge, or to give over the booty merely.
flashlight cop \n (n.)\n

(US police) a security guard.

2005J. Buskey Tinged Valor 40: This was totally offensive to a veteran officer, ranking right up there with being referred to as a \xe2\x80\x98flashlight cop\xe2\x80\x99 (security guard).
horse cop \n (n.)\n

(US) a police officer riding a horse.

1952Lait & Mortimer USA Confidential 302: Horse cops are a common sight all over midtown New York.
2001Lost Trailers Tour Journal 26 July At one point I did steal a quick glance behind me and saw a horse cop shining his flashlight around the parking lot.
soft cop \n (n.)\n [note the trad. hard cop/soft cop interrogation routine]

a gullible, well-meaning person, e.g. a community/social worker whose sympathies can be exploited.

2001Guardian 19 Mar. Thanks to Madd, no more soft cop for DUIs. If you test positive it\xe2\x80\x99s handcuffs, the drunk tank and big-stick punishment.

\n In exclamations\n

cop bung! [fig. use of bung v.1 (4)]

(UK Und.) look out! the police are coming!

1857\xe2\x80\x98Ducange Anglicus\xe2\x80\x99 Vulgar Tongue 38: A cross-cove, who had his regulars, called out \xe2\x80\x98cop bung\xe2\x80\x99.
1859Matsell Vocabulum 17: cap [sic] bung Hand it over; give it to me.
1882Sydney Sl. Dict. 10/1: Jack buzzed a bloak and a shickster of a reader and a skin. A cross-cove, who had his regulars for stalling, cried \xe2\x80\x98Cop bung,\xe2\x80\x99 as a pig was marking. Jack speeled to the crib. Jack picked the pockets of a gentleman and a lady of a pocket-book and a purse. A fellow-thief, who had his share of the plunder for watching, cried \xe2\x80\x98Hand over the property,\xe2\x80\x99 as someone was observing. Jack ran away home.
1889\xe2\x80\x9390Barrère & Leland Dict. of Sl., Jargon and Cant.
