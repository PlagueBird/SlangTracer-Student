rabbit n.2

1. a wooden drinking vessel.

1685G. Meriton In Praise of York-shire Ale 1: Strong Beer in Rabits and cheating penny Cans.
c.1698B.E. Dict. Canting Crew n.p.: Rabbits, Wooden Kanns to Drink out of, once used on the Roads, now almost laid by.
1725New Canting Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1737, 1759, 1760, 1776Bailey Universal Etym. Eng. Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue n.p.: Rabbits were also a sort of wooden canns to drink out of, now out of use.
1811Lex. Balatronicum [as cit. 1785].
1823Egan Grose\xe2\x80\x99s Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1963R. McGregor-Hastie Compleat Migrant 108: Rabbit, to rum the: to fetch beer from a public house.

2. (US/Aus., also bunny) a bottle of beer.

1895C. Crowe Aus. Sl. Dict. 64: Rabbit, [...] a bottle of beer.
1910O. Johnson Varmint 299: Dish out the bunny.
1941Baker Popular Dict. Aus. Sl. 58: Rabbit, a bottle of beer.
1955N. Pulliam I Travelled a Lonely Land (1957) 237/2: rabbit \xe2\x80\x93 [...] sometimes a bottle of beer.

\n In phrases\n

run the rabbit \n (v.)\n (Aus.) \n

1. to bring home liquor from a public house; thus rabbit-runner, one who carries out this errand.

1895Coburg Leader (Vic.) 15 June 1/6: Billy H., of Coburg, runs the rabbit\npretty often.
1898Bendigo Indep. (Vic.) 22 Jan. 6/5: His \xe2\x80\x98push\xe2\x80\x99 who for the whole of Friday and late into Friday night were \xe2\x80\x98running the rabbit\xe2\x80\x99 (carry beer in bottle or billy-can).
1908Morwell Advertiser (Vic.) 4 Dec. 4/1: In one of the public offices there used to be messenger who was pretty frequently sent for beer, to \xe2\x80\x98run the rabbit,\xe2\x80\x99 as the saying is.
1910Bulletin (Sydney) 4 Aug. 10/1: \xe2\x80\x98Do you mix your drinks with water? Do you house a demijohn? / Do you ever run the rabbit? Do you like it cold, or hot? / Have you got the sipping habit? / Are you sober, please, or not?\xe2\x80\x99 [Ibid.] 22 Sept. 10/1: He also \xe2\x80\x98runs the rabbit,\xe2\x80\x99 and may be seen at certain times of the day cantering out from among his green-stuffs with a drinking vessel in either hand [...] Snowball, M.L.A., cold-tea apostle, represents the Brighton Chows in Parliament; but, as Snowball doesn\xe2\x80\x99t know a rabbit-runner when he sees one, he can hardly be blamed for permitting the presence of these offensive Chow-encouraging beer-houses in his constituency.
1915C.J. Dennis \xe2\x80\x98The Intro\xe2\x80\x99 in Songs of a Sentimental Bloke 22: Not since I wus a tiny little cub, / An\xe2\x80\x99 run the rabbit to the corner pub.
1922Call (Perth) 27 Oct. 2/6: \xe2\x80\x98Running the rabbit\xe2\x80\x99 in Perth is not confined to the \xe2\x80\x98humble beer jug,\xe2\x80\x99 as a close watch of a well-known Beaufort-street hostelry would easily demonstrate.
1942Cairns Post (Qld) 31 Mar. 1/4: Sir\xe2\x80\x94In to-day\xe2\x80\x99s quiz you state that \xe2\x80\x98to run the rabbit\xe2\x80\x99 means in Australian language, to fetch beer from a hotel. Well, you may be right, but in the bush we call it \xe2\x80\x98running the cutter\xe2\x80\x99.

2. to obtain liquor illegally.

1941Baker Popular Dict. Aus. Sl. 62: Run the rabbit, to secure liquor, often illegally, e.g. after hours.
1955N. Pulliam I Travelled a Lonely Land (1957) 238/1: run the rabbit \xe2\x80\x93 to buy liquor illegally after closing hours or in some zone supposed to be closed.
