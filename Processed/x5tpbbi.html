crib v.1

[? SE crib, a small wickerwork container, poss. used by a poacher]

1. to indulge in petty theft; to take surreptitiously; thus cribbing n.

1732Harlot\xe2\x80\x99s Progress 34: Rejoicing at a Watch she crib\xe2\x80\x99d / The Night before, from one that nib\xe2\x80\x99d.
1758R. Dodsley King and Miller of Mansfield 19: He cribs, without Scruple, from other Men\xe2\x80\x99s Sacks.
1772Foote Nabob in Works (1799) II 298: There are a brace of birds and a hare, that I cribbed this morning out of a basket of game.
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue n.p.: To Crib, to purloin, or appropriate to one\xe2\x80\x99s own use, part of anything entrusted to one\xe2\x80\x99s care.
1795Sporting Mag. Nov. VII 81/1: We would niver have cribb\xe2\x80\x99d your papers.
1811Lex. Balatronicum [as cit. 1785].
1822Westmorland Gaz. 7 Sept. 4/5: There were to be found [...] knowing-ones [...] and those who entrap the most scientific, either at cribbing a tattler or a fawney.
1833Mr Mathews\xe2\x80\x99 Comic Annual 22: Each one suspects his neighbour of bribery; / Each thinks t\xe2\x80\x99other cribs, / By planning, the dibs, / And truth when asserted, is thought to be fibs.
1836\xe2\x80\x937Dickens Pickwick Papers (1999) 422: Child, being fond of toys, cribbed the necklace.
1843Satirist & Sporting Chron. (Sydney) 1 Apr. 2/3: Happiest men \xe2\x80\x94 if, cribbing votes / With all thy wonted powders of blarney.
1842Wkly Rake (NY) 24 Sept. n.p.: Monsieur \xe2\x80\x98cribbed\xe2\x80\x99 a piece of cheese \xe2\x80\x94 he did like cheese .
1845R.S. Surtees Hillingdon Hall I 264: [of a speech] I\xe2\x80\x99m a goin\xe2\x80\x99 to do a bit of antiquity myself \xe2\x80\x94 cribbed of course, but that\xe2\x80\x99s nothin\xe2\x80\x99.
1848Flash Dict. in Sinks of London Laid Open.
1850\xe2\x80\x98Ned Buntline\xe2\x80\x99 G\xe2\x80\x99hals of N.Y. 139: To leave it, would be a certain loss to you, for, as I said before, the stars would be sure to crib it.
1865Potter Jrnl (Coudersport, PA) 25 Jan. 1/4: On a starvation salary the temptation to crib from the change drawer is often too strong.
1869J. Greenwood Seven Curses of London 407: It is not stealing [...] this tearing fourteen stamps from a sheet at which everybody in the office has access, and which will be replaced without question as soon as it is exhausted. It is at most only \xe2\x80\x98cribbing\xe2\x80\x99.
1874S. Baker Ismailia II 252: No thefts had been allowed, not even those trifling annexations of property which are distinguished from stealing by the innocent name of \xe2\x80\x98cribbing\xe2\x80\x99.
1885Bulletin (Sydney) 18 Apr. 20/1: The writers in a newspaper office in Melbourne had a vigorous hunt for a Russian lately [...] and with the view to cribbing a little local colour, an intelligent quill-driver was sent out to talk the situation over with one of the Russian residents of the city.
1888St Paul Dly Globe 28 July 10/6: The above-mentioned comrades [...] did on or about July 19 [...] enter the chicken coop of a comrade and did [...] steal, purloin, rob, crib, cabbage, abstract, or otherwise carry away, eleven chickens.
1892 \xe2\x80\x98Man-Trap\xe2\x80\x99 in \xe2\x80\x98F. Anstey\xe2\x80\x99 Mr Punch\xe2\x80\x99s Model Music Hall 94: He\xe2\x80\x99s always cribbing coppers \xe2\x80\x93 which he spends on lollipops.
1895Crowe Aus. Sl. Dict. 20: Crib, to steal.
1900Boy\xe2\x80\x99s Own Paper 1 Dec. 132: We hadn\xe2\x80\x99t cribbed Rawbone\xe2\x80\x99s money.
1910\xe2\x80\x98Doss Chiderdoss\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Games For Girls\xe2\x80\x99 Sporting Times 20 Aug. 1/3: Schoolgirls crib the schoolboys\xe2\x80\x99 games.
1917Mt Vernon Signal (KY) 2 Nov. 4/3: It shall be unlawful to pick up [...] steal, seize, [...] pilfer, purloin [...] crib, cabbage [...] another man\xe2\x80\x99s umbrella.
1929(con. 1916) F. Manning Her Privates We (1986) 24: \xe2\x80\x99E\xe2\x80\x99s been cribbin\xe2\x80\x99 everything.
1940M. Marples Public School Slang 8: Stealing or appropriating [...] crib .
1960, 1975Wentworth & Flexner DAS.

2. to take some money out of one\xe2\x80\x99s wages or an amount set aside for essentials.

1748Dyche & Pardon New General Eng. Dict. (5th edn) n.p.: Crib to withhold, keep back, pinch, or thieve a part out of money given to lay out for necessaries.
1869 \xe2\x80\x98Rights of Women\xe2\x80\x99 in C. Hindley Curiosities of Street Lit. (1871) 82: You must not crib a shilling from your wages on Saturday night.

3. to obtain a new recruit for the services; the implication is by using trickery.

1879H. Hartigan Stray Leaves (2nd ser.) 63: Very queer things were in vogue when he was a young fellow to crib men for the service.
