rattle v.

1. to send away.

1624Massinger Parliament of Love II i: leonora: Slaue, beate him back [...] servant: Madam I rattled him, Rattled him home. leonora: Rattle him hence you rascall Or never see me more.
a.1625Beaumont & Fletcher Love\xe2\x80\x99s Cure III iii: He was here [...] but I ratled him; I told him here was no Companions For such debauch\xe2\x80\x99d, and poor-condition\xe2\x80\x99d Fellows.

2. to unnerve, to frighten; thus rattling adj.; also vtr. to lose one\xe2\x80\x99s nerve, composure.

1633Cottington To Strafford in Hallam Const. Hist. II, 89: The king hath so rattled my lord-keeper that he is the most pliable man in England [F&H].
1669Pepys Diary 25 Mar. n.p.: So I did lay the law open to them, and rattle the Master-Attendants out of their wits almost.
1694Congreve Double-Dealer II i: I\xe2\x80\x99ll rattle him up, I warrant you, I\xe2\x80\x99ll firk him with a certiorari!
1715T. Hearne Reliquiae (1857) 331: Townshend, one of the secretaries of state, hath sent rattling letters to Dr. Charlett .
1785Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue n.p.: To rattle one off; to rate or scold him.
1869J.R. Browne Adventures in Apache Country 282: I think he was slightly rattled by the formidable appearance of our escort.
1884Kansas Cowboy 23 Aug. in Miller & Snell Why the West was Wild 614: The city marshal brought up a six-footer of monstrous proportions, who got into a dive, and was rattling up the establishment.
1896W.C. Gore Student Sl. in Cohen (1997) 21: rattle v. To confuse, embarrass.
1908\xe2\x80\x98O. Henry\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98The Clarion Call\xe2\x80\x99 in Voice of the City (1915) 195: Kind of rattles you, doesn\xe2\x80\x99t it, to have the mysterious villain call up [...] and tell you what a helpless old gas-bag you are?
1910Sun. Times (Perth) 12 June 2nd sect. 10/2: He says his game of talk helped win the fight, as it got Nelson \xe2\x80\x98rattled\xe2\x80\x99.
1919H.C. Witwer Smile A Minute 135: I got up amidst the wildest applause and four shells from the German trenches. Neither of \xe2\x80\x99em rattled me.
1925Wodehouse Carry on, Jeeves 37: He took it without blinking. You can\xe2\x80\x99t rattle Jeeves.
1934Wodehouse Right Ho, Jeeves 175: This little spot of friction had rattled the bearded bloke a bit.
1936\xe2\x80\x98Banjo\xe2\x80\x99 Paterson Shearer\xe2\x80\x99s Colt 67: It takes much to rattle a Chinaman.
1947I. Shulman Amboy Dukes 65: If he became rattled they were goners.
1959D. Niland Big Smoke 153: Don\xe2\x80\x99t let it rattle you, Jacky.
1976P. Theroux Family Arsenal 99: Mayo shrugged, but the talk had rattled her.
1983A. Payne \xe2\x80\x98Get Daley!\xe2\x80\x99 Minder [TV script] 51: You really think he doesn\xe2\x80\x99t know who rattled Harrison?
1997C. Hiaasen Lucky You 180: She slipped behind the wheel of a giant Ford sedan, which momentarily rattled Shiner.
2000Guardian Guide 5\xe2\x80\x9311 Feb. 52: We can only hope that part two sees him grasp the chance to rattle him into defending his essentially Thatcherite ideas.
2012T. Piccirilli Last Kind Words 33: [H]e couldn\xe2\x80\x99t meet my eyes. He rattled way too easy.

3. to have sexual intercourse.

1640Parliament of Women B4: After spake Mistris Rachel Rattle a pace [etc].
[1661 in Ebsworth Merry Drollery Compleat (1875) 77: If Venus and Mars so stout / Had joined together in battle, / There could not have been more claps and more bangs, / For he made her old buttocks rattle].
1725View of London & Westminster (2nd part) 44: [in a list of prostitutes] Miss Rattle [Is Visited] By Lord Belisarius.
1966Trimble 5000 Adult Sex Words and Phrases.
2005P. Howard Curious Incident of the Dog in the Nightdress 4: Rattling his sister was payback.
2018I. Welsh Dead Man\xe2\x80\x99s Trousers [15]: I never rattled as many women in my life as I did when I was married.

4. (also rattle along, rattle off, rattle one\xe2\x80\x99s ass, ...hocks) to leave, to move off, usu. quickly and noisily.

c.1698B.E. Dict. Canting Crew n.p.: Rattle c. to move off, or be gone.
1725New Canting Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1737, 1759, 1760, 1776Bailey Universal Etym. Eng. Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1794Sporting Mag. May IV 124/1: Lud, Sur, I\xe2\x80\x99ve been every where, / Round the globe have rattled.
1811Lex. Balatronicum.
1847\xe2\x80\x98Epistle from Joe Muggins\xe2\x80\x99s Dog\xe2\x80\x99 in Era (London) 24 Jan. 4/2: Away they rattled, right and left.
1853G.J. Whyte-Melville Digby Grand (1890) 20: We rattled along by moonlight on our way to the barracks.
1863T. Taylor Ticket-Of-Leave Man Act III: I\xe2\x80\x99ll drive to the bank \xe2\x80\x94 cash this \xe2\x80\x94 settle with those counter-skippers, and rattle back in time to see you turned off.
1865Leaves from Diary of Celebrated Burglar 70/2: In a minute we were on our way to Edinburgh. As we rattled along we turned out the \xe2\x80\x98poke\xe2\x80\x99.
1895Record-union (Sacramento, CA) 30 Nov. 6/4: They come off the train [...] ready to rattle their hocks for the hotel.
1910Keowee Courier (SC) 13 Apr. 2/2: Everybody rattle your hocks.
1914C. Holme Lonely Plough (1931) 144: Lup said, \xe2\x80\x98Like enough! Good-night to you, sir!\xe2\x80\x99 and rattled off.
1933M. Marshall Tramp-Royal on the Toby 58: Along I rattled at a great pace.
1934(con. 1880s) E. Cunningham Triggernometry (1957) 209: Nor was it so simple as \xe2\x80\x98rattling their hocks\xe2\x80\x99 out of the American Valley.
1972(con. 1916) G. Swarthout Tin Lizzie Troop (1978) 196: Was he, Dinkle, rattling his ass, and theirs, into a trap.

5. to move about, to act with energy, thus rattling, noisly energetic.

c.1775H. Carey Honest Yorkshire-Man 18: O Bartledom [i.e. Bartholemew] Fair / [...] / Such Pork, Such Pig, / Such Game, Such Rig, / Such Rattling there.
1786J. O\xe2\x80\x99Keeffe Wicklow Mountains 16: In town I was gay; I rattled, swore, guzzled and gambled.
1840London Mag. Feb. 14/2: The kingdom of Kerry had the honour of giving birth to this excellent specimen of a \xe2\x80\x98rattling, roaring Irishman;\xe2\x80\x99.
1842S. Lover Handy Andy 83: It\xe2\x80\x99s I that\xe2\x80\x99ll rattle you there in no time, your honour.
1847Era (London) 6 June 5/1: Werattled into Windsor to have breakfast.
1903A.H. Lewis Boss 98: Shadow him: swing and rattle with him no matter where he goes.
1925Black Mask Aug. III 49: The cops [...] hurried out to the Street as a patrol came rattling up.
1963G. Lambert Inside Daisy Clover (1966) 220: I could get up right this moment, and rattle around the house for a while.
1997Share Slanguage.

6. (Ulster) to work energetically.

1996Share Slanguage.

7. to hit someone; thus rattle the ivories v., to hit someone in the teeth.

1861M.E. Braddon Trail of the Serpent 315: The gentlemen of the prize-ring were prepared to fight as long as they had a bunch of fives to rattle upon the knowledge-box of the foe.
1907Carr & Chase \xe2\x80\x98Word-List From Eastern Maine\xe2\x80\x99 in DN III:iii 248: rattle one\xe2\x80\x99s slats, v.phr. To give a blow or blows in the ribs. \xe2\x80\x98He rattled his slats.\xe2\x80\x99.
1919Marvel 3 Mar. 5: Hammer him! Go it, Bodder! Rattle it into the pig! Lam him!
1984P. Barker Blow Your House Down 5: You want to rattle her chops a bit. That\xe2\x80\x99d sharp cure her.
2018I. Welsh Dead Man\xe2\x80\x99s Trousers [13]: [Y]ou\xe2\x80\x99re no getting rattled. Or even hurt.

8. (US Und.) to blackmail.

1949Monteleone Criminal Sl. (rev. edn).
1950Goldin et al. DAUL 175/1: Rattle. To extort money from.

9. (drugs) to tremble, from heroin addiction.

1999Scotland on Sun. Mag. 7 Nov. 16: 95% of Glasgow\xe2\x80\x99s prostitutes are injecting drug addicts. That\xe2\x80\x99s why they sway [...] That\xe2\x80\x99s why they tremble. Rattling they call it. They need a hit.
2012(con. 1980s) I. Welsh Skagboys 168: Ah\xe2\x80\x99m a wee bit sick. \xe2\x80\x94Rattlin? [Ibid.] 238: There\xe2\x80\x99 still a bit of a drought up the road, skag-wise [...] I called Matty the other day. he was rattling like as pandas in a Chinese takeaway.

\n In compounds\n

rattle-wagon \n (n.)\n

(Aus.) a ricketty, rattling vehicle.

1912Eve. News (Sydney) 24 Oct. 6/4: [T]axpayers are of necessity compelled to travel by rattle-waggon, bone-box, or brakevan, on business or pleasure, wholly destitute of seating accommodation.

\n In phrases\n

rattle along \n (v.)\n

see sense 4 above.

rattle off \n (v.)\n

1. (also rattle up) to scold, to tell off.

1549T. Chaloner (trans.) Erasmus Praise of Folie (1509) 103: I toke not vpon me to boult out the maner of bishops and priestes liuyng now a daies: lest any man shoulde deme how I rather entended to ratle vp theyr vices, than to spredde myne owne praise.
1694Congreve Double-Dealer II i: Sir Paul, have patience; let me alone to rattle him up.
c.1698B.E. Dict. Canting Crew n.p.: I Rattled him, I Rate [i.e. rated] him roundly, and told him his own.
1725New Canting Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1731Chickens Feed Capons 13: She [...] sate up for me her self, out of mere Spight and pure Intention to rattle me off.
1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue (2nd, 3rd edn).
1811Lex. Balatronicum.
1823Egan Grose\xe2\x80\x99s Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.

2. see sense 4 above.

rattle one\xe2\x80\x99s ass \n (v.)\n

rattle one\xe2\x80\x99s cage \n (v.)\n

1. to effect emotionally, e.g. annoy, irritate, nag, intimdate.

1976J.W. Dean III Blind Ambition 249: I think it\xe2\x80\x99s time I rattled the cage a little bit and let them know the cover-up is really over.
1995G. Pelecanos Down by the River 208: \xe2\x80\x98I just like to rattle that redneck\xe2\x80\x99s cage a little bit, that\xe2\x80\x99s all\xe2\x80\x99.
1999D. Lehane Prayers for Rain 186: I\xe2\x80\x99ve had my cage rattled by pros, though--wiseguys, cops, gang-bangers.
2011Sun. Times (Johannesburg) 30 Nov. [headline] Nando\xe2\x80\x99s rattling Robert Mugabe\xe2\x80\x99s Cage [...] Nando\xe2\x80\x99s, whose latest advertisement depicts the ageing authoritarian president.
2020A. Parks Bobby March Will Live Forever 239: \xe2\x80\x98Tried to rattle his cage but he was having none of it. Stayed ice cold\xe2\x80\x99.

2. to make a fuss.

2002J. Cameron Hell on Hoe Street 60: Teach them some fucking manners, throw a fucking wobbly soon as they rattle their cage.
rattle one\xe2\x80\x99s hocks \n (v.)\n

rattle someone\xe2\x80\x99s chain \n (v.)\n [var. on jerk someone\xe2\x80\x99s chain under jerk v.2 )]

(orig. US) to annoy, to distract forcefully, to taunt.

1992J. Mowry Way Past Cool 7: Just make it shoot again an stop rattlin my goddam chain, huh!
rattle the cup on \n (v.)\n

(US) to betray, to inform against.

1935A.J. Pollock Und. Speaks n.p.: Rattle the cup, an inmate making a noise on cell bars to attract attention of guard, often to give information about another prisoner.
1949Monteleone Criminal Sl. (rev. edn).
1959J. Thompson Getaway in Four Novels (1983) 74: Doc couldn\xe2\x80\x99t split with her or let her split with him. She\xe2\x80\x99d be too apt to rattle the cup on him. Buy herself a deal at his expense.
