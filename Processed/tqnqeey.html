twist v.

1. to hang, to be hanged; thus as excl. twist me!\n [lit./fig. twisting on the rope].

1725New Canting Dict. n.p.: His Colquarron is just about to be twisted. He is just going to be turn\xe2\x80\x99d off.
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1809J. Mackcoull Abuses of Justice 33: Nothing short of my death should appease him, and of that he was now certain, for that I should as surely be twisted.
1811Lex. Balatronicum n.p.: The kiddy dropped down when he went to be twisted; the young fellow was very low spirited when he walked out to be hanged.
1827Egan Anecdotes of the Turf, the Chase etc. 182: harry might either be lagged or twisted.
1830Lytton Paul Clifford III 246: Poor Harry was twisted shortly after.
1834W.A. Caruthers Kentuckian in N.Y. I 29: And, twist me, if I didn\xe2\x80\x99t feel as if I was about to be nicked.
1859 \xe2\x80\x98Scene in a London Flash-Panny\xe2\x80\x99 Matsell Vocabulum 100: If I wished to nose I could have you twisted.
1895Crowe Aus. Sl. Dict. 90: Twisted [...] hanged.

2. in senses of \xe2\x80\x98twisting the rules\xe2\x80\x99.

(a) to steal; thus twisting n.

1865Leaves from Diary of Celebrated Burglar 147/2: We learned that Tom Kay had been engaged in \xe2\x80\x98fencing\xe2\x80\x99 some \xe2\x80\x98broady\xe2\x80\x99 that had been \xe2\x80\x98twisted\xe2\x80\x99 for.
1868 \xe2\x80\x98Six Years in the Prisons of England\xe2\x80\x99 in Temple Bar Mag. Nov. 537: When you go into any place where hats, coats, or umbrellas are left in the lobby, you can take a new \xe2\x80\x98tog\xe2\x80\x99, or a new hat, by mistake for your own. This is \xe2\x80\x98twisting,\xe2\x80\x99 or ringing the changes.
1890Bulletin (Sydney) 6 Sept. 24/1: The last man they sent me as a clerk was a swell mobsman, lagged for twisting a sparkler.* [...] [*\xe2\x80\x98Lagged for twisting a sparkler\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x93 transported for stealing diamond rings from a jeweller\xe2\x80\x99s counter while inspecting other goods with a pretended view to purchase].

(b) to cheat, to defraud; thus twisting n.

1853Chester Chron. 25 June 6/5: If the \xe2\x80\x98coppers\xe2\x80\x99 or police officers are too wide-awake [...] the twisting dodge [i.e defrauding a shopkeeper when changing a large note] is tried.
1906W. Boyle Eloquent Dempsy (1911) Act I: I wonder does he see the way you\xe2\x80\x99re twisting him \xe2\x80\x93 you rogue?
1906Sun. Times (Perth) 16 Sept. 4/7: Up I come before the beak, not knowin\xe2\x80\x99 she \xe2\x80\x99ad twisted on me, an\xe2\x80\x99 done a get.
1914Jackson & Hellyer Vocab. Criminal Sl. 95: They had to learn awareness in the school of cold, hard facts, having been [...] \xe2\x80\x98twisted\xe2\x80\x99 [...] times innumerable.
1929\xe2\x80\x98Henry Green\xe2\x80\x99 Living (1978) 284: It\xe2\x80\x99s wicked, Joe, \xe2\x80\x99er\xe2\x80\x99s twistin\xe2\x80\x99 yer.
1937C. Drew \xe2\x80\x98Order of the Mallet\xe2\x80\x99 in Bulletin 28 Apr. 4/2: \xe2\x80\x98It\xe2\x80\x99d be funny,\xe2\x80\x99 I says to Handsome, \xe2\x80\x98if Gorilla was to twist on us.\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Why would he?\xe2\x80\x99 says Handsome. \xe2\x80\x98He\xe2\x80\x99s gettin\xe2\x80\x99 sixty per cent, of the chop-up, ain\xe2\x80\x99t he?\xe2\x80\x99.
1938R. Chandler \xe2\x80\x98Red Wind\xe2\x80\x99 in Red Wind (1946) 54: I never had a chance to twist one like I got tonight.
1941Baker Popular Dict. Aus. Sl.
1956\xe2\x80\x98Charles Raven\xe2\x80\x99 Und. Nights 167: I couldn\xe2\x80\x99t afford to cut up rough unless I caught him in the act of twisting me.
1966B. Reckord Skyvers I ii: They don\xe2\x80\x99t know twistin\xe2\x80\x99. They don\xe2\x80\x99t know gamblin\xe2\x80\x99 and swearin\xe2\x80\x99.

(c) to lie.

1925S. O\xe2\x80\x99Casey Juno and the Paycock Act III: Did you ever know me to twist; did you ever know me to twist?

3. (UK Und.) to steal a watch by first snapping and twisting off its ring; thus twisting adj.

1865Leaves from Diary of Celebrated Burglar 7/1: A song was called for by old \xe2\x80\x98Kill-the-Bull,\xe2\x80\x99 a veteran of the \xe2\x80\x98twisting school.\xe2\x80\x99.
1904\xe2\x80\x98Number 1500\xe2\x80\x99 Life In Sing Sing 253: Twist. to snap the ring of a watch. [Ibid.] 257: Super-Twister. Pickpocket who steals watches.

4. (Aus.) to be convicted of a crime.

1895C. Crowe Aus. Sl. Dict. 90: Twisted, convicted.
1941Baker Popular Dict. Aus. Sl.
1990Tupper & Wortley Aus. Prison Sl. Gloss. Twist. [...] to be convicted of a crime.

5. (US, also twist a burn) to roll a cigarette.

1913B.T. Harvey \xe2\x80\x98Word-List From The Northwest\xe2\x80\x99 in DN IV:i 28: twist, v. To roll a cigarette. \xe2\x80\x98Twist me one.\xe2\x80\x99.
1944P. Kendall Dict. Service Sl. n.p.: twist a dizzy . . . roll a cigaret.
1947\xe2\x80\x9353W. Guthrie Seeds of Man (1995) 258: I couldn\xe2\x80\x99y hold my han\xe2\x80\x99 still enough right now ta twist up anuther cigarette.
1950Goldin et al. DAUL 230/1: Twist one. (P) To roll a cigarette.
1955N. Pulliam I Travelled a Lonely Land (1957) 241/1: twist a burn \xe2\x80\x93 roll a cigarette.
1971\xe2\x80\x932C. Shafer \xe2\x80\x98Catheads [...] and Cho-Cho Sticks\xe2\x80\x99 in Abernethy Bounty of Texas (1990) 217: twist one up, v. \xe2\x80\x93 to roll a cigarette.
1997Eble Campus Sl. Fall.

6. (drugs) to roll a marijuana cigarette.

1942Berrey & Van den Bark Amer. Thes. Sl.
1949Monteleone Criminal Sl. (rev. edn) 245: twist a dream To roll a cigarette; to roll a marihuana cigarette.
1973D. Goines Street Players 7: You can twist me another, as long as you\xe2\x80\x99re at it, man.
1997Eble Campus Sl. Fall.
2000Ebonics Primer at www.dolemite.com twist Definition: to roll and smoke a blunt (joint). Example: Yo, chill, nigga...lay back and twist one.

7. to leave.

1957C. MacInnes City of Spades (1964) 97: Oh, blow, man [...] Twist now \xe2\x80\x94 you dig?

twist (down) (apace) \n (v.)\n [? the twisting of the wrist in eating or tearing pieces of bread from a loaf; note Worcestershire dial. twist something down one, to eat heartily]

to eat, esp. to eat heartily.

c.1698B.E. Dict. Canting Crew n.p.: Twist, to Eat. To Twist lustily, to Feed like a Farmer.
1725New Canting Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1737, 1759, 1760, 1776Bailey Universal Etym. Eng. Dict. [as cit. c.1698].
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue n.p.: To twist it down apace; to eat heartily.
1811Lex. Balatronicum [as cit. 1785].
1823Egan Grose\xe2\x80\x99s Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1827 \xe2\x80\x98Moderate Appetite\xe2\x80\x99 in Dibdin\xe2\x80\x99s Vocal Gleaner 40: He can twist down for dinner five pounds of roast beef.
