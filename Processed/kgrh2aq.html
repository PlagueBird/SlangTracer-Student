brickfielder \n (n.)\n [orig. a thick cloud of dust brought over Sydney, New South Wales, by a south wind from neighbouring sandhills (called the \xe2\x80\x98brickfields\xe2\x80\x99)] (Aus.)

1. in Sydney, a sudden squally wind, bringing relief at the end of a hot day although sometimes accompanied by a dust-storm.

1829Sydney Monitor 10 Oct. 2/6: His Excellency and family were placed in considerable danger whilst sailing in consequence of a brickfielder [...] which nearly capsized the boat [AND].
1833Lt. Breton, R.N. Excursions in New South Wales and Van Diemen\xe2\x80\x99s Land 293: It sometimes happens that a change takes place from a hot wind to a brickfielder, on which occasions the thermometer has been known to fall, within half an hour, upwards of fifty degrees!
1844J. Rae Sydney Illus. 26: The \xe2\x80\x98brickfielder\xe2\x80\x99 is merely a colonial name for a violent gust of wind, which, succeeding a season of great heat, rushes in to supply the vacuum, and equalises the temperature of the atmosphere.
1852G.C. Mundy Our Antipodes I 46: [of Sydney] At length comes the expected \xe2\x80\x98Brickfielder,\xe2\x80\x99 drifting the pulverized abominations into every pore of the human frame.
1863F. Fowler Athenaeum 21 Feb. 264 I: The brickfielder is not the hot wind at all; it is but another name for the cold wind or southerly buster, which follows the hot breeze, and which, blowing over an extensive sweep of sandhills called the Brickfields, semi-circling Sydney, carries a thick cloud of dust (or brickfielder) across the city.
1898E.E. Morris Austral Eng. 52/2: Brickfielder. [...] The brickfields lay to the south of Sydney, and when, after a hot wind from the west or north-west, the wind went round to the south, it was accompanied by great clouds of dust, brought up from the brickfields.
1915N. Duncan Aus. Byways 130: A southerly buster would blow \xe2\x80\x93 a Sydney brickfielder.
1982N. Keesing Lily on the Dustbin 128: \xe2\x80\x98The brickfielder\xe2\x80\x99, a blustery southerly wind that once blew grit and dust far and wide over infant Sydney.
2018G. Gilmore Base Nature [ebook] The breeze comes again, stronger this time and somehow even dryer than before. It is a brickfielder straight from the desert, like opening an oven door.

2. a hot, dusty wind that blows over parts of northern Australia.
