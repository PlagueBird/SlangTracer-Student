jack n.2

[abbr. SE jackass]

1. a fool.

c.1525J. Rastell Gentleness and Nobility line 239: Nay, iwys, pyvsh and rude Jack Javell.
1577Misogonus in Farmer (1906) II i: Marry. sir! this Jack Prat will go boast.
1593Shakespeare Taming of the Shrew II i: To wish me wed to one half lunatic; A madcap ruffian and a swearing Jack.
1607Dekker & Webster Westward Hoe IV i: There be a thousand bragging Jackes in London.
1617J. Taylor \xe2\x80\x98Iacke a Lent\xe2\x80\x99 in Works (1869) I 120: If any poore Iacke-a-Lent doe happen into the hands of a foole, tis but a Foole and a Iacke, or two fooles well met.
1640H. Mill Nights Search I 52: For I do use her, as a hooke and line To catch Iack-simple.
1641\xe2\x80\x9374 \xe2\x80\x98The Merry Country Maid\xe2\x80\x99s Answer\xe2\x80\x99 in Ebsworth Roxburghe Ballads (1891) VII:2 341: For rather than I\xe2\x80\x99le marry such a Clownish Jack, / I\xe2\x80\x99le buy a witty fellow cloath[e]s to put on \xe2\x80\x99s back.
1659Catterpillers of this Nation Anatomized 5: Its likely they are but lately rose from that doglike life of Iackyes like the fool that is proud of his own wit.
1675C. Cotton Scoffer Scoff\xe2\x80\x99d (1765) 221: This Jack, / This paltry Mountebanking Quack.
1948P. Kavanagh Tarry Flynn (1965) 97: \xe2\x80\x98Shut up, you jack, you,\xe2\x80\x99 said Mary.
1989H. Rawson Dict. of Invective (1991) 14: The insulting connotations usually come through most clearly when the familiar form of a name is used, as in Chico, Charlie, Heinie, Hymie, Jack, Mick, Paddy.
2000Indep. Rev. 3 Mar. 10: A jack named Vig [...] a dopey redneck who wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t know responsibility if it palled up with him in a bar.

2. (US Und.) a drunk individual who is made the victim of a theft.

1908Dly Press (Newport News, VA) 19 Apr. 12/14: Once in a whilea pickpkcket will run across a drunken man who is easy to rob. Such a man is known as a \xe2\x80\x98jack\xe2\x80\x99.

\n In compounds\n

jack act \n (v.)\n [SE jackass]

(Irish) to play the fool; thus jack-acting n.

1925S. O\xe2\x80\x99Casey Juno and the Paycock Act III: Come on, out with th\xe2\x80\x99 money, an\xe2\x80\x99 don\xe2\x80\x99t be jack-actin\xe2\x80\x99.
1952B. MacMahon Children of the Rainbow 126: Stop your jack-actin\xe2\x80\x99.
1969P. Boyle All Looks Yellow to the Jaundiced Eye 61: A group of schoolboys comes trampling down, shouting and jack-acting.

\n In phrases\n

lay on the jack \n (v.)\n

to beat or scold severely.

1568Hist. of Jacob and Esau V vi: If I wrought one stroke to day, lay me on the iacke.
1579North Lives of Noble Grecians 127: They should make no reckoning of all that brauery and brags, but should sticke to it like men, and lay it on the iacks of them.
play the jack \n (v.)\n

1. to act the villain. [play on jack = the \xe2\x80\x98knave\xe2\x80\x99 in cards].

1611Shakespeare Tempest IV i: Your fairy [...] has done little better than play the jack with us.
1613Rowlands \xe2\x80\x98A Drunken Knave\xe2\x80\x99 Knave of Hearts 53: Boy y\xe2\x80\x99are a villaine, didst thou fill this sacke? / \xe2\x80\x99Tis flat you Rascall, thou hast plaid the Jacke.
1668Pepys Diary 23 Feb. n.p.: Sir R. Brookes overtook us coming to town; who played the jack with us all, and is a fellow that I must trust no more.

2. to play the fool.

1984Partridge DSUE (8th edn) 897/1: C.19.
