fly n.3

[pun on bluebottle n. (2)]

1. a police officer.

1852Kendal Mercury 17 Apr. 6/1: Vell, ven they are going off, the flies (police) begins to tumble (understand) to it.
1857J. Archbold Magistrate\xe2\x80\x99s Assistant n.p.: A fly, A policeman.
c.1863 gloss. in Occurence Book of York River Lockup in Seal (1999) 37: A cross cove who had his regulars lowr, a fly grabbed him. I am afraid he will blow it.
1889Clarkson & Richardson Police! 320: A policeman ... A fly, Jack, B.D., slip, crusher, peeler, body-snatcher, raw lobster, tin ribs, stalk, danger signal, terror etc.
1901Wichita Dly Eagle (KS) 24 Apr. 4/3: De guy\xe2\x80\x99s wife gits next to him an\xe2\x80\x99 hires some flys to shadder him, so she can put de Judge wise an\xe2\x80\x99 git her fifty per, alimony.
1904Sun (NY) 10 July 29/4: Here is a genuine letter written in thieves\xe2\x80\x99 slang, recently found by the English police [...] The noise of the milling the glass brought tray flies. She chucked a reeler and was lugged before the beak and fine[d] a bull.
1959I. & P. Opie Lore and Lang. of Schoolchildren (1977) 395: Nicknames current among boys [...] Fly, Flatfoot.

2. see fly cop n. (1)

3. see botfly n. (2)

[1889Windsor & Richmond Gaz. flybog \n (n.)\n [flies that land on jam tend to get stuck]

(Aus.) treacle, jam.

1918Aussie (France): Aus. Soldiers\xe2\x80\x99 Mag. Feb. 6/2: \xe2\x80\x98Back in the waggon lines we get butter, rooty, rice an\xe2\x80\x99 flybog, but \xe2\x80\x99ere on the dump, if yer get butter yer don\xe2\x80\x99t get fly bog, an\xe2\x80\x99 if yer get flybog yer don\xe2\x80\x99t get butter\xe2\x80\x99.
1923Northern Miner (Charters Towers, Qld) 6 Jan. 7/3: A meal which consisted [...] of porridge, flybog and tea.
1931Sydney Mail 14 Oct. 2/4: You'll find some more damper in there, flybog, cocky\xe2\x80\x99s joy, bullocky\xe2\x80\x99s delight, axle grease, and a bit o\xe2\x80\x99 junk. That day we had some doughboys and \xe2\x80\x98underground mutton\xe2\x80\x99.

fly-catcher \n (n.)\n

1. a gawping fool [his open mouth].

1653Urquhart (trans.) Gargantua and Pantagruel (1927) I Bk I 103: The bunsellers or cake-makers [...] did injure them most outrageously, calling them [...] ninnie-hammer fly-catchers [...] and other such like defamatory epithets.
1801G. Hangar Life, Adventures and Opinions II 59: Ye lovely Cyprians, never hire a young fly-catching foot-boy [...] but keep a stout, sturdy young fellow.

2. the vagina [see prev.].

1890\xe2\x80\x931904Farmer & Henley Sl. and Its Analogues.
fly cemetery \n (n.)\n [joc. \xe2\x80\x98resemblance\xe2\x80\x99]

1. a currant cake.

1915Watchman (Sydney) 12 Aug. 3/3: [I]nstead of asking for a sausage roll, \xe2\x80\x98Tommy\xe2\x80\x99 will call for a \xe2\x80\x98torpedo,\xe2\x80\x99 a twopenny meat pie is known as a \xe2\x80\x98shell,\xe2\x80\x99 and a currant cake is a \xe2\x80\x98fly cemetery\xe2\x80\x99.
1923St George Call (Kogarah, NSW) 5 Oct. 3/6: \xe2\x80\x98Yes, we will have no bananas\xe2\x80\x99 at the Ref\xe2\x80\x99s Outing, but there will be fly-cemeteries and cocoanut dough-nuts.

2. a pastry square filled with mincemeat.

1921Fort Scott Dly Trib. (KS) 16 Aug. 5/2: I cannot decide what of all the many [Scottish] cakes and goodies I like best, but think it is the \xe2\x80\x98Fly Cemetary\xe2\x80\x99 .
1970Partridge DSUE (7th edn) 1141: since ca. 1945.
1988McGill Dict. of Kiwi Sl. 46/1: fly cemetery dried fruit mix in pastry sandwich, usually currants, sometimes called a squashed fly cemetery if sultanas; in English boarding schools a currant pudding.
1990Honolulu Star-Advertiser (HI) 9 Nov. D6/4: \xe2\x80\x98Fly Cemetery,\xe2\x80\x99 the New Zealand name for raisin pie.
1991M. Binchy Circle of Friends 173: Could the thought of my company [...] and the distinct possibility that I would buy you a coffee and a fly cemetery make you change your mind?
1999M. Lipman Lip Reading 118: Already we were back in the dinner queue, the only difference being it was chicken and fruit salad instead of tinned mince and fly cemetery.
1999Baltimore Sun (MD) 22 Sept. F1/4: [Scottish food feature] A fruit slice (raisins, currants and crushed almonds between two layers of sugar-sprinkled pastry) known as a \xe2\x80\x98fly cemetery\xe2\x80\x99.
2003McGill Reed Dict. of N.Z. Sl. [as cit. 1988].
2010News Jrnl (Wilmington, DE) 22 Sept. 27/1: Fly Cemetery. Dolly wrote, \xe2\x80\x98I just love this recipe\xe2\x80\x99 which originated in Scotland. The \xe2\x80\x98flies\xe2\x80\x99 can be currants, rasins or both.

3. (N.Z.) a raisin biscuit.

1980L. Leland Personal Kiwi-Yankee Dict.
1988N. Virtue Then Upon the Evil Season 104: Pita ate another fly cemetery. Then he sat, looking queasy.

4. steamed pudding with currants.

1963New Society 22 Aug. 37/2: The well-known fly cemetery and frogspawn are still ok \xe2\x80\x93 descriptions of currant puddings and tapioca.

5. (Irish) a currant bun.

2001G. Coughlan Everyday Eng. and Sl. Fly Cemetery (n): currant bun.
2005Y. Collins Introducing Vivian Leigh Reid 30: \xe2\x80\x98Try the fly cemetery,\xe2\x80\x99 he suggests. I stare into his eyes, transfixed, before offering this conversational gem: \xe2\x80\x98What?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98The currant buns,\xe2\x80\x99 he says.

flytrap \n (n.)\n

1. the mouth, esp. a large one [note trap n.1 (5)].

1797 M.G. Lewis in Sporting Mag. June X 174/1: The bride shuts her fly trap; the stranger complies.
1834\xe2\x80\x98Jack Downing\xe2\x80\x99 Andrew Jackson 121: The Arrah Nows, with their fly-traps open, wou\xe2\x80\x99d toss in a plum.
1843N.Y. Sporting Whip 4 Mar. n.p.: You gave that fly-trap of yours such a twist.
1848E. Bennett Mike Fink 9/1: Jest keep that ugly fly-trap o\xe2\x80\x99 yourn shut.
1851 \xe2\x80\x98How Sally Hooter Got Snake-Bit\xe2\x80\x99 in T.A. Burke Polly Peablossom\xe2\x80\x99s Wedding 71: I shut up my fly trap, an\xe2\x80\x99 lay low an\xe2\x80\x99 kep dark!
1862J.A. Hardwick \xe2\x80\x98Cheap John\xe2\x80\x99 Prince of Wales\xe2\x80\x99 Own Song Book 50: Shut up your fly-traps and listen to Cheap John.
1881Sacramento Dly Record (CA) 24 Dec. 1/1: \xe2\x80\x98Pass the coffin varnish this way, Lieutenant.\xe2\x80\x99 [...] \xe2\x80\x98Coffin vanrish indade. I\xe2\x80\x99d have you understand...\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Oh, shut up that fly-trap\xe2\x80\x99.
1913J. London Valley of the Moon (1914) 25: If I\xe2\x80\x99d knowed you was Bill Roberts there wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t been a peep from my fly-trap.
1917\xe2\x80\x98Henry Handel Richardson\xe2\x80\x99 Aus. Felix (1971) 25: You keep your fly-trap shut, my fine fellow.
1935A.J. Pollock Und. Speaks.
1965W. Alfred Hogan\xe2\x80\x99s Goat I iv: A Lutheran lawyer with a fly-trap mouth.
1977T. Berger Who is Teddy Villanova? 10: \xe2\x80\x98Shut your fucking flytrap,\xe2\x80\x99 he growled.
1982M. Braun Judas Tree (1983) 27: Close your flytrap and make tracks.

2. an inferior, prob. corrupt gambling club or casino.

1827Guards 51: [T]he common hells, the rookeries and pigeon- holes, cobweb warehouses and fly-traps, where the Greek committee sits nightly.

3. a run-down hotel or similar establishment.

1909\xe2\x80\x98O. Henry\xe2\x80\x99 Options (1916) 62: Old Jerome was lingering long after breakfast [...] before setting forth to his down town fly-trap.
1922\xe2\x80\x9324O.O. McIntyre White Light Nights 23: The tarnished \xe2\x80\x98Flytrap\xe2\x80\x99 could not compete with the mirrored elegance of a tea dansant.
1935O.O. McIntyre \xe2\x80\x98New York Day by Day\xe2\x80\x99 11 June [synd. col.] The cheapest of all jags [...] was a seidel of beer through a straw. It\xe2\x80\x99s still a custom in Bowery fly-traps.
1944J. Archibald \xe2\x80\x98Downed on the Farm\xe2\x80\x99 in Ten Detective Aces Nov. You mean Poultney\xe2\x80\x99s Paradise-in-the-Pines? [...] I would have to be paid to return to that fly trap.

4. see fly-cage

flytrapper \n (n.)\n

a blow to the mouth.

1842Era 11 Dec. 10/2: After going it seemingly hammer and tongs for an hour and a half, all the sanguinary evidence [...] was a cut over the eye of the Slasher, and a ditto with a flytrapper on the mug of Brother Jonathan.
fly-up-the-creek \n (n.)\n [regional use fly-up-the-creek, a popular name of the small green heron (Butorides virescens), a native of Florida] (US)

1. a poor white.

1927(con. mid-19C) E. Rowland Varina Howell 92: [She] regarded with contempt the Democrats whom she had heard derided as \xe2\x80\x98Sandhills,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Hill Billies,\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Fly-up-the-creeks,\xe2\x80\x99 and even \xe2\x80\x98the Dirt Eaters;\xe2\x80\x99 and always these were referred to as \xe2\x80\x98the poor whites of the State\xe2\x80\x99 [i.e. Mississippi].

2. an inhabitant of Florida.

1866Montana Post (Virginia City, MT) 28 Apr. 4/1: The inhabitants of [...] Florida [are called] Fly-up-the-Creeks.
1873Jasper Wkly Courier (IN) 28 Nov. 8/1: Delaware Musk rats; Florida, Fly-up-the-Creeks; Goergia, Buzzards; [...] Maine, Foxes.
1885North Amer. Rev. Nov. 433: Among the rank and file, both armies, it was very general to speak of the different States they came from by their slang names. Those from Maine were called Foxes; [...] Missouri, Pukes; Mississippi, Tad Poles; Florida, Fly up the Creeks; Wisconsin, Badgers; Iowa, Hawkeyes; Oregon, Hard Cases.

3. a capricious person; also as adj., foolish.

1893in DARE.
1903DN II.
1915M.G. Hayden \xe2\x80\x98Terms Of Disparagement\xe2\x80\x99 in DN IV:iii 220: fly-up-the-creek, foolish.
1926Wood & Goddard Dict. Amer. Sl.

4. an immoral woman.

1966in DARE.
