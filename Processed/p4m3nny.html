pot v.

[all other senses are fig. use of sense 2, where the food goes in the SE pot]

1. to outdo, to outwit, to deceive; thus potter, a trickster.

1848Era (London) 17 Sept. 5/2: I was pleased as punch to see the Nobblers and Potters done.
1855T. Taylor Still Waters Run Deep II ii: A greater flat was never potted.
1880E.J. Milliken \xe2\x80\x98Cad\xe2\x80\x99s Calendar\xe2\x80\x99 Punch Almanack Feb. n.p.: Crab your enemies, I\xe2\x80\x99ve got a many, / You can pot \xe2\x80\x99em proper for a penny.
1894Coburg Leader (Vic.) 3 Nov. 2/4: They Say [...] The Potters met the Lyndhurst second match last season and took them down. Clay boys say they will again \xe2\x80\x98Pot\xe2\x80\x99 them.

2. to shoot, esp. food for eating; thus potting n.

1860Chambers\xe2\x80\x99s Journal xiii 90: A few... amuse themselves by potting at us, but they are in too great a state of fear to make good practice [F&H].
1861T. Hughes Tom Brown at Oxford (1880) 451: That doesn\xe2\x80\x99t include turning out to be potted at like a woodcock.
1874\xe2\x80\x98Old Calabar\xe2\x80\x99 Won in a Canter I 81: \xe2\x80\x98Shooting landlords like the devil. Potted Lambert last week\xe2\x80\x99.
1875\xe2\x80\x98Aliph Cheem\xe2\x80\x99 Lays of Ind (1905) 11: \xe2\x80\x98I raised my gun to pot him [i.e. a tiger] and my hand was on the trigger\xe2\x80\x99.
1880Bulletin (Sydney) 19 June 1/2: There is hardly a man in North Queensland whose motto is not \xe2\x80\x98See a nigger and “pot him\xe2\x80\x99.
1883J. Keane On Blue Water 145: One of the boys [...] wanted to know if a soldier, after he was shot, wasn\xe2\x80\x99t potted lobster.
1884Ally Sloper\xe2\x80\x99s Half Holiday 8 May 7/2: He speared and potted each derned cuss / As he chanced to meet.
1885 \xe2\x80\x98\xe2\x80\x99Arry on the Merry Month of May\xe2\x80\x99 Punch 16 May 229/1: Day\xe2\x80\x99s rabbittin\xe2\x80\x99s not a bad barney, and gull-potting\xe2\x80\x99s lummy, no doubt.
1885L. Emanuel Jottings [...] of a Bengal \xe2\x80\x98qui hye\xe2\x80\x99 18: This brave but rash oficer [...] was deliberately \xe2\x80\x98potted\xe2\x80\x99.
1886I.D. Hardy Oranges and Alligators 30: A third has \xe2\x80\x98potted\xe2\x80\x99 an alligator, about five feet long.
1888\xe2\x80\x98Rolf Boldrewood\xe2\x80\x99 Robbery Under Arms (1922) 343: He could see to fire at a top window close by where the doctor and Mr. Knightley had been potting at us.
1892H. Nisbet Bushranger\xe2\x80\x99s Sweetheart 301: The dingo almost missed me, but it was my rifle which potted him.
1898Boy\xe2\x80\x99s Own Paper 12 Nov. 103: Has everyone got catties and shot with them? [...] They might be very useful for potting at the masters on the drive.
1901Marvel XV:390 Apr. 10: I warn you and your men to sheer off, and if you are not on the return journey in three seconds, we\xe2\x80\x99ll pot you!
1902\xe2\x80\x98Dads Wayback\xe2\x80\x99 in Sun. Times (Sydney) 16 Nov. 5/4: \xe2\x80\x98[E]f yer crops failed [...] Yous just loaded up yer gun an\xe2\x80\x99 started out an\xe2\x80\x99 potted, er cove whose crop wos better\xe2\x80\x99.
1905H. Green Actors\xe2\x80\x99 Boarding House (1906) 102: Fat Belly potted it.
1913D. Runyon \xe2\x80\x98Mexican Love Song\xe2\x80\x99 5 Nov. [synd. col.] I love to [...] dream of pleasant scenes / Like pottin\xe2\x80\x99 burglin\xe2\x80\x99 jockeys from the stands.
1915\xe2\x80\x98Ian Hay\xe2\x80\x99 First Hundred Thousand (1918) 202: Their snipers go potting way all night, but they don\xe2\x80\x99t often get anybody.
1915L.D. Richards diary 9 May It\xe2\x80\x99s a wonder we weren\xe2\x80\x99t potted off, as most of the time we could be seen by the enemy.
1919E. Dyson \xe2\x80\x98Mickie Mollynoo\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98Hello, Soldier!\xe2\x80\x99 44: I pots the extra one; / Mick chokes his third in comfort.
1921(con. WWI) E. Lynch Somme Mud 37: I\xe2\x80\x99ll pot a Fritz every chance I get.
1926S. O\xe2\x80\x99Casey Plough and the Stars Act II: Supposin\xe2\x80\x99 I happened to be potted?
1931D. Runyon \xe2\x80\x98Dream Street Rose\xe2\x80\x99 Runyon on Broadway (1954) 57: Do not think it will do you any good to pot me in the back when I turn around.
1941P. Gallico Snow Goose 42: [British speaker] We was roostin\xe2\x80\x99 on the beach between Dunkirk an\xe2\x80\x99 Lapanny, like a lot o\xe2\x80\x99 bloomin\xe2\x80\x99 pigeons on Victoria Hembankment, waitin\xe2\x80\x99 for Jerry to pot us. \xe2\x80\x98E potted us good too.
1940O. Strange Sudden Takes the Trail 90: He would \xe2\x80\x98a\xe2\x80\x99 tried to pot me.
1948R. Park Harp in South 82: Wild boys and girls who had specialized in potting king\xe2\x80\x99s Men from behind hedges.
1953\xe2\x80\x98Curt Cannon\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98The Death of Me\xe2\x80\x99 I Like \xe2\x80\x99Em Tough (1958) 100: As far as Grafton knows, he potted the right pigeon.
1954Wodehouse Jeeves and the Feudal Spirit 41: With Riffs or whatever they\xe2\x80\x99re called potting at him from all directions.
1961M. Braly Felony Tank (1962) 98: They didn\xe2\x80\x99t even see me. They were all too busy trying to pot you guys.
1992R.G. Barrett White Shoes 154: You can\xe2\x80\x99t [...] take on half a dozen terrorists armed with machine-guns like you\xe2\x80\x99re goin\xe2\x80\x99 out pottin\xe2\x80\x99 rabbits.
2001A. Sillitoe Birthday 130: He craved a three-o-three Short Lee Enfield service rifle to pot one of the tyres.

3. to punish.

1885Bulletin (Sydney) 23 May 6/2: In the libel suit of Dove v. the London Referee is perhaps the choicest example of what juries will do when they lose their heads or there\xe2\x80\x99s a newspaper to be \xe2\x80\x98slated.\xe2\x80\x99 […] Dove bought an action and \xe2\x80\x98potted\xe2\x80\x99 the paper for £300.

4. to appropriate.

1884Ally Sloper\xe2\x80\x99s Half Holiday 24 May 25: [caption] Iky Mo\xe2\x80\x99s last words to Ally were \xe2\x80\x94 \xe2\x80\x98Give anything to one, as long as you pot the stake\xe2\x80\x99.
1908\xe2\x80\x98Doss Chiderdoss\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98When Love Began\xe2\x80\x99 Sporting Times 8 Feb. 1/3: He\xe2\x80\x99d no claim to be the only bird she\xe2\x80\x99d potted; / It transpired that other love-birds had been victims of her sport, / And they all had been successfully small shotted.

5. to take from, to extort.

1885Bulletin (Sydney) 1 Aug. 4/4: And the \xe2\x80\x98Queen gave the bride away.\xe2\x80\x99 There was magnanimity for you! To give her child away to a poor young man and pot the nation for the \xe2\x80\x98dot!\xe2\x80\x99.
1915Bulletin (Sydney) 2 Sept. 26/1: What gave me the rift in the gizzard was that one of the mob booted the carpet off me arf-a-quid lid, and another potted a new stook and four jim that I was goin\xe2\x80\x99 to weigh out for me new clobber.

6. (Aus./N.Z.) to throw a stone.

2003McGill Reed Dict. of N.Z. Sl. 162: pot [...] 2. To throw a stone, shortened version of pot-shot.

7. (US) to hit, to strike.

1909Gem 23 Jan. 18: He meant to pot your hat, but he might easily have potted your brain-box by mistake!

8. (Aus./N.Z.) in Und. uses.

(a) to arrest; to charge.

1897Independent (Footscray. Vic.) 17 July 3/3: I am going to be potted for this, if people didn\xe2\x80\x99t give me drink I wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t do such things.
1901Bulletin (Sydney) 12 Oct. 13/3: A well-known desperado [...] was said to be mixed up in various cowardly garotting episodes, but all the police could pot him for was insulting behavior, or something of that kind.
1912Bulletin (Sydney) 17 Oct. 12/2: A Melbourne carpenter, who pleaded guilty and was committed for trial, had the bad luck to be \xe2\x80\x98potted\xe2\x80\x99 last week on the charge of falsely representing himself as a victim of the recent railway accident, whereas he had escaped a nervous shock by missing the train.
1919Sun. Times (Perth) 15 June 1/6: [headline] Blackguards Both Two Parasites Potted.
1934Frankston & Somerville Standard (Vic.) 7 Dec. 4/4: He heard Roogerson demand to be taken to the police station, and heard Constable Kofoed tell him to go home. He had no desire to see Rogerson \xe2\x80\x98potted\xe2\x80\x99.

(b) to inform against, to hand over for trial.

1900Bulletin (Sydney) 19 May 11/1: \xe2\x80\x98Thank God that chap was potted,\xe2\x80\x99 said the gaoler as the pious embezzler was found guilty. \xe2\x80\x98We badly want an organist down at the prison!\xe2\x80\x99.
1902Bulletin (Sydney) 27 Sept. 29/2: An\xe2\x80\x99, roundin\xe2\x80\x99 the kips, / Yer kin pump \xe2\x80\x99em, an\xe2\x80\x99 then / Yer off ter the demons / To sell what yer got, / When yer through / Wif a few / Ter squeal on an\xe2\x80\x99 pot.
1914Sport (Adelaide) 14 Aug. 4/6: Martin\xe2\x80\x99s ragtackers are sending [...] to find out who potted them for riding the man\xe2\x80\x99s bike.
1921Advertiser (Adelaide) 18 Feb. 8/5: I know who has potted on me.
1937Advocate (Burnie, Tas.) 27 Oct. 6/5: Witness seized the liquor and emopty bottles Horton said, \xe2\x80\x98You have been laid on to this,\xe2\x80\x99 and Stokes said, \xe2\x80\x98Yes, who potted us?\xe2\x80\x99.
1973(con. 1930s) F. Huelin \xe2\x80\x98Keep Moving\xe2\x80\x99 22: \xe2\x80\x98If you hadn\xe2\x80\x99t put on that damn fool act we wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t be here,\xe2\x80\x99 I snapped at darky. \xe2\x80\x98Ther kid would have potted us anyway,\xe2\x80\x99 he retorted defensively.
2003McGill Reed Dict. of N.Z. Sl. 162: pot [...] 3. To inform on.

(c) (Aus.) to jail, to sentence.

1914Truth (Melbourne) 21 Feb. 12/6: Soon they pots him for a sixer, / Doin of a burglary.

9. to render drunk.

1947I. Shulman Amboy Dukes 10: The zombie really had potted him.

10. (S.Afr.) to drink.

1977C. Hope Ducktails in Gray Theatre Two (1981) 51: jimmie: What you potting in there? bo-bo: Brandy. jimmie: Give you a terrible babalas, Bo-bo.

11. of a man, to seduce, to have sexual intercourse with.

1998G. Burn Happy Like Murderers 200: [Her father] potted two of his daughters.

pot off \n (v.)\n

(US) to kill.

1937T. Brennan Death Squads in Morocco 38: Four or five such creeping figures and we should be potted off before we knew anything about it.
1956H. Ellison \xe2\x80\x98Johnny Slice\xe2\x80\x99s Stoolie\xe2\x80\x99 Deadly Streets (1983) 86: They wouldn\xe2\x80\x99t pot off the dummy.
