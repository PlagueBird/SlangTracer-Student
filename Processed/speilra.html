cracker n.1

[SE crack/crack n.3 (4)]

1. (UK Und.) the backside.

1674Head Canting Academy (2nd edn).
c.1698B.E. Dict. Canting Crew n.p.: Cracker, c. an Arse.
1725New Canting Dict.
1737, 1759, 1760, 1776Bailey Universal Etym. Eng. Dict.
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1811Lex. Balatronicum.

2. (S.Afr.) in pl., sheep-skin trousers.

1833C.G.H. Literary Gazetter 2 Sept. 238: Old Crackers alias leather breeches.
1845Autobiog. of Sir Harry Smith II 348: You [...] would laugh to see our motley group, with every costume of a mean kind [...] the 72nd\xe2\x80\x99s men with crackers.
1889H.A. Bryden Kloof and Karroo 199: Klaus looked pretty well \xe2\x80\x98baked\xe2\x80\x99 even in his old leather \xe2\x80\x98crackers\xe2\x80\x99.
1913C. Pettman Africanderisms 131: Crackers Trousers of prepared sheep-skin, largely used in the early days by the settlers, and so named because of the cracking noise which they made at every move of the wearer.
