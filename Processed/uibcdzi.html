crib n.2

[SE crib, a container for animal fodder]

1. the stomach.

1641R. Brome Jovial Crew II i: Here\xe2\x80\x99s Pannam and Lap, and good Poplars of Yarrum, / To fill up the Crib, and to comfort the Quarron.

2. provisions; also attrib.

1700N. Ward \xe2\x80\x98A Frolic to Horn-Fair\xe2\x80\x99 in Writings (1704) 209: With as much indignation and Revenge, as a She-Mumper, when Bilk\xe2\x80\x99d of her crib.
1833 \xe2\x80\x98The Cly-Pecker\xe2\x80\x99 Swell!!! or, Slap-Up Chaunter 38: The tipp\xe2\x80\x99ry was ask\xe2\x80\x99d for the crib, and agree\xe2\x80\x99d.
1953T.A.G. Hungerford Riverslake 21: \xe2\x80\x98Where can I go to look for him?\xe2\x80\x99 \xe2\x80\x98In the crib-room,\xe2\x80\x99 one of the men at the bench voluteered.

3. (Aus./N.Z.) a snack, a light meal, a piece of bread, cake etc; thus crib bag, crib break, crib room, cribtime.

1870Old Hunks in Darkey Drama 5 53: harry: I\xe2\x80\x99m witness that you promised to give Tommy something better than a crib. tommy: A good round dinner.
1900\xe2\x80\x9310Stephens & O\xe2\x80\x99Brien Materials for a Dict. of Aus. Sl. [unpub. ms.] 52: CRIB: miners term for a twenty-minute interval for food or smoke-ho, known as crib-time; and lunch carried to work is known as crib.
1906E. Dyson \xe2\x80\x98A Saturday at Spat\xe2\x80\x99s\xe2\x80\x99 in Fact\xe2\x80\x99ry \xe2\x80\x99Ands 123: She [...] spoke a three minutes grace over a \xe2\x80\x98crib\xe2\x80\x99 of bread and jam that it took her two minutes to eat.
1926K.S. Prichard Working Bullocks 105: The bullockies and swampers threw themselves under trees near the landing for crib, at midday [...] they sat there drinking black tea and eating their dry bread and sandwiches.
1967 \xe2\x80\x98Whisper All Aussie Dict.\xe2\x80\x99 in Kings Cross Whisper (Sydney) xxxiii 4/4: crib: The food taken to work. Not so much a popular expression in the city.
1973(con. 1930s) F. Huelin \xe2\x80\x98Keep Moving\xe2\x80\x99 49: Bring your cribs and be on time.
1993Aus. Word Map crib [...] \xe2\x80\x98My father who was a bus driver in Perth from the 1940s to the 1970s used to take 'his crib' to work, like other drivers. This was his meal to be had during his main break\xe2\x80\x99 [...] \xe2\x80\x98 the word CRIB which I understand to mean mid day meal (as used in Broken Hill) comes from a cornish [...] origin [...] the container which one used to carry ones crib in which was a cane or wickerware basket which was called a cribbage\xe2\x80\x99 .
