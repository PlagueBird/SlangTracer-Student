chip n.2

[? gambling chips; note racing jargon chip, one shilling; Indian army jargon chip, a rupee]

1. a dollar.

1840Spirit of the Times (N.Y.) 11 Apr. 61: A [...] horse [....] on whom he is perfectly willing to \xe2\x80\x98pile the chips\xe2\x80\x99 for any distance [HDAS].
1857 \xe2\x80\x98Fighting the Tiger\xe2\x80\x99 in S.F. Call 26 Mar. n.p.: All the browns his uncle lent him, / All the chips and dust and clinkers.
1877Sun (Concord, NC) 6 Mar. 3/4: K, of course, was a few \xe2\x80\x98chips\xe2\x80\x99 short and tried [...] borrowing from Benny.
1895Manchester Courier 23 Mar. 14/2: The Red Gulch \xe2\x80\x98Snorter\xe2\x80\x99 of Arizona is a breezy journal. Here is its announcement of terms:\xe2\x80\x94 \xe2\x80\x98Any galoot who wants the “Snorter for a year can have it [...] on payment of three red chips in advance\xe2\x80\x99.
1899\xe2\x80\x931900C.L. Cullen Tales of the Ex-Tanks 79: I\xe2\x80\x99m going to spin a few chips on him [i.e. a racehorse].
1905\xe2\x80\x98Hugh McHugh\xe2\x80\x99 You Can Search Me 18: I\xe2\x80\x99m a few chips shy myself on account of a side play [...] I took a few slices of Amalgmated Copper and burned my thumb.
1922L.A. Times 4 May sect. II 4: Herewith another installment of snappy younger generation slang just broadcast from eastern points, where it has its origin: [...] Boffos: Dollars, likewise known as rocks, seeds, berries, chips or jack.
1960, 1975 (ref. to 1920s) Wentworth & Flexner DAS.

2. (mainly US) in pl., money; often in fig. phrs. below.

1836 \xe2\x80\x98Saint Peter\xe2\x80\x99s Lips\xe2\x80\x99 in Frisky Vocalist 28: Come, old chap, pray tip the chips.

1853Dickens \xe2\x80\x98Slang\xe2\x80\x99 in Household Words 24 Sept. 75/2: Money \xe2\x80\x93 the bare, plain, simple word itself [...] might have sufficed, yet we substitute for it \xe2\x80\x93 tin, rhino, blunt, rowdy, stumpy, dibbs, browns, stuff, ready, mopusses shiners, dust, chips [etc.].
1857S.F. Call 26 Mar. n.p.: [He] Went to fight the furious tiger, / Went to fight the beast at faro, / And was cleaned out so completely / That he lost his every mopus, / Every single speck of pewter, / Every solitary shiner, / Every brad and every dollar [...] All the dibs he did discover, / All the browns his uncle lent him, / All the chips and dust and clinkers.

3. £1, a sovereign.

1859 \xe2\x80\x98Hundred Stretches Hence\xe2\x80\x99 in Matsell Vocabulum 124: The chips, the fawneys, chatty-feeders, / The bugs, the boungs, and well-filled readers.

6. (US black) a nickel, five cents.
