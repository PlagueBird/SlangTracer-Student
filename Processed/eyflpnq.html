rabbit n.1

1. in sexual senses.

(a) a prostitute.

1579S. Gosson School of Abuse (1868) 35: If this were well noted, as ill seene [...] I haue no doubt but the cause would be feared to dry vp the effect, and these pretty Rabbets very cunningly ferretted from their borrowes.
1812J. Bell Jr. (ed.) Rhymes of Northern Bards 254: Each cuddles his coney or rabbit, / And pleasantly purrs with puss-cats; / Hence with husky harlots cohabit, / And handle a herdling\xe2\x80\x99s old hats.
1830Owl (NY) 25 Sept. n.p.: Who has got them Rabbits, and what have they done with them, they skinned them.

(b) (Aus.) a girl.

1900\xe2\x80\x9310Stephens & O\xe2\x80\x99Brien Materials for a Dict. of Aus. Sl. [unpub. ms.] 129: Young girls in \xe2\x80\x98push\xe2\x80\x99 circles are known as \xe2\x80\x98rabbits.\xe2\x80\x99.

(c) (S.Afr.) a male homosexual.

(d) a client who ejaculates quickly and thus leaves the prostitute free to carry on her trade.

1971L. Sanders Pleasures of Helen 85: \xe2\x80\x98A rabbit,\xe2\x80\x99 she said softly. \xe2\x80\x98A goddamned rabbit. Fast? An Olympic champ. And the grunts!\xe2\x80\x99.

(e) (US gay) a fellator.

2. a term of abuse.

3. a newborn baby; used only in combs., such as rabbit-catcher and rabbit-snatcher [an affectionate nickname, but ? ref. to one Mary Tofts (c.1701\xe2\x80\x9363) who, in 1726, allegedly (but fraudulently) \xe2\x80\x98gave birth\xe2\x80\x99 to a litter of rabbits].

rabbit-sucker \n (n.)\n

1. (UK Und.) a rich young man who is gulled into running up large bills by confidence tricksters who later dun him for his debts.

c.1597Shakespeare Henry IV Pt 1 II iv: Hang me up by the heels for a rabbit sucker.
1608Dekker Lanthorne and Candle-Light Ch. 4: The Cittizen that selles them is the Ferret. They that are take up are the Rabbit-suckers. He upon whose credit these Rabit-suckers runne, is called the Warren.
c.1698B.E. Dict. Canting Crew n.p.: Rabbet-suckers, young Unthrifts taking up Goods upon Tick at excessive rates.
1725New Canting Dict. n.p.: rabbet-suckers young Unthrifts taking up Goods upon Tick of Pawn-brokers, or Tally-men, at excessive Rates.
1737, 1759, 1760, 1776Bailey Universal Etym. Eng. Dict. [as cit. 1725].
1785, 1788, 1796Grose Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue n.p.: Rabbit suckers, Young spendthrifts taking up goods on trust at great prices.
1811Lex. Balatronicum [as cit. 1785].
1823Egan Grose\xe2\x80\x99s Classical Dict. of the Vulgar Tongue.
1859Matsell Vocabulum.

2. one who lends at exorbitant rates, thus rendering impoverished those to whom they extend credit.

1725New Canting Dict. n.p.: Rabbet-sucker, [...] rabbet-suckers [...] a Name given to Pawn-Brokers and Tally-men themselves, who by exorbitant Premiums, &c. impose upon, and take-in uncautious Prodigals, profuse Spendthrifts, or young giddy-headed Heirs; and in this Sense is an applicable Denomination to many other sorts of People, besides those above-mentioned, who by lending Money at exorbitant Rates, to profuse Raking Youths, under Age, whose careful Parents or Guardians will not furnish them with Means to support their Extravagancies; by little and little, creep into their Estates; so that by the Time the unwary Spend-thrift comes of Age, or to inherit his Patrimony, he finds himself and his Fortune swallowed up by these merciless Rabbet, or rather Blood-suckers, and so turn\xe2\x80\x99d out of Possession, and reduced to Penury and Want, which oftentimes hurries him into Desperation and violent Practices, and perhaps brings him finally to an untimely Exit.
