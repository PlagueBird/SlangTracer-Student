fly n.2

[SE fly, i.e. one is \xe2\x80\x98flying a kite\xe2\x80\x99]

1. a trick, a dodge.

1891A. Day Mysterious Beggar 271: I put it to him steep; and, my boy, [...] he riz to the fly!

2. (UK und.) constr. with the, any crime involving skill rather than brute force.

1882\xe2\x80\x98Some Varieties of Thieves\xe2\x80\x99 in Star (London) 23 Feb. 4/2: The wide doman known among light-fingered gentry as \xe2\x80\x98the fly\xe2\x80\x99.

3. (N.Z. prison) one who borrows money or other goods without repaying.

2001D. Looser Boobslang [U. Canterbury D.Phil. thesis] 71/2: fly n. = seagull.

\n In phrases\n

on the fly

1. begging by following passers-by and asking for cash, rather than standing in one place.

1861 (con. 1840s\xe2\x80\x9350s) H. Mayhew London Labour and London Poor II 51/2: The \xe2\x80\x98first move\xe2\x80\x99 in his mendicant career was taking them on the fly; which means meeting the gentry on their walks, and beseeching or at times menacing them till something is given.
1881Dundee Courier (Scot.) 25 Feb. 6/5: Brighton is a first-rate place for \xe2\x80\x98copping them on the fly\xe2\x80\x99 (begging from people in the streets).

2. getting one\xe2\x80\x99s living by theft, prostitution or some other form of crime.

1859, 1860Hotten Dict. of Modern Sl. etc.
1864, 1867, 1870Hotten Sl. Dict.
1873Sl. Dict.
1895C. Crowe Aus. Sl. Dict. 54: On the Fly, getting one\xe2\x80\x99s living by thieving.
1931D. Runyon \xe2\x80\x98Earthquake\xe2\x80\x99 in Runyon on Broadway (1954) 146: Sam is on the fly at the time.
