tiger n.

1. with ref. to individuals.

(a) an overdressed, showy man.

1830Egan Finish to the Adventures of Tom and Jerry (1889) 74: I met the Corinthian, the other day [...] amongst all the Tigers of note, the Lions, and other Great Creatures belonging to the Menageries of Fashion.
1834Trumpeter General (Hobart, Tas.) 19 Aug. 3/6: In Englaii a solitary individual attempts to introduce a new of carriage, or shape of hat, is caricatured, and becomes what is called, in terms of slang, a tiger.
1849Thackeray Pendennis I 185: A man may have a very good coat of arms, and be a tiger, my boy [...] that man is a tiger, mark my word \xe2\x80\x93 a low man.
1887A.C. Gunter Mr Barnes of N.Y. 294: This English tiger will not suspect his bride.

(b) (also tiger-boy, a smartly dressed manservant, esp. a boy who accompanies his master in his coach; (Aus.) a groom (often black).

1828Lytton Pelham II 70: I sent my cab-boy (vulgo Tiger) to inquire of the groom, whether the horse was to be sold.

2. Prentiss, was stumping the country, and came to a town where there was a small menagerie on exhibition.
